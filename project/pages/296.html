TCL 40FS3800

  • Pros

    Inexpensive. Roku TV features. Good shadow detail.

  • Cons

    Colors appear slightly undersaturated. Awkward port placement.

  • Bottom Line

    The TCL FS3800 Roku TV series is an affordable line of connected HDTVs with solid performance.

By Will Greenwald

If you want an inexpensive HDTV with built-in Wi-Fi and online features like Netflix and Hulu Plus, you want a Roku TV. The best budget HDTVs we've tested over the last year have been Roku TVs, all sharing Roku's menu system despite coming from different HDTV manufacturers. The FS3800 series is another Roku TV line from TCL, similar to the FS3700 series we reviewed earlier this year. It's functional, offers accurate colors and solid contrast, and at $339 for the 40-inch 40FS3800 we tested, it's very affordable. The Editors' Choice Insignia NS-DR420NA16 series stays on top with just slightly more vibrant colors and better contrast, but it's a close race among all the Roku TVs we've seen so far.

Design
The 40FS3800 looks very plain and simple, like the 48FS3700. The screen is surrounded by a half-inch glossy black bezel angled slightly inward to evoke a picture frame, with a silver-colored accent running along the bottom edge. The HDTV sits on two curved plastic feet that hold the screen steady.

View All 7 Photos in Gallery

Oddly, all three of the 40FS3800's HDMI ports face down from the back of the HDTV, along with a 3.5mm headphone jack and a cable/antenna connector. A USB port, optical audio output, composite video input, and pinhole Reset button face left from the back of the screen. Side ports are easier to access than down- or back-facing ports, and it's odd not to see at least one HDMI port on the side. It's a small inconvenience, but not a big problem once you have all of your devices set up, unless you often switch between more than three HDMI video sources. A four-way navigation pad that doubles as a Power button sits on the lower right corner of the back of the screen.

Roku TV remotes are as close to standardized as any HDTV remotes have ever been. All of the Roku TVs we've seen from TCL, Hisense, and Sharp have shared nearly identical pill-shaped remote controls, with only details like quick-access buttons and volume control placement differentiating them. The 40FS3800 remote has the same big purple direction pad for controlling the HDTV as other remotes we've seen, along with a handful of playback and navigation buttons. Individual quick-access buttons for Netflix, Amazon Instant Video, Rdio, and Vudu sit on the lower half of the remote. Volume Up/Down and Mute buttons sit on the right edge, difficult to see but easy to find with fingers.

Roku TV
As a Roku TV, the 40FS3800 offers identical features to other Roku TVs like the Hisense 40H4, the Insignia NS-55DR420NA16, and the Sharp LC-43LB371U. The interface is based on Roku's menu system, and it can access streaming media services and other apps through Roku's Channel Store. All of the major services are available, like Netflix, Hulu Plus, Amazon Instant Video, YouTube, Twitch, and Sling TV. Additionally, more subject-specific content channels for various movie genres, sports, regions, languages, and subjects can be found for free or for a premium through the Roku Channel Store.

The 40FS3800 supports control through the free Roku app for iOS and Android. It lets you use your smartphone or tablet as a remote control, to input text, and even to stream movies, music, or pictures from your mobile device to the HDTV.

Performance
We test HDTVs with a Klein K-10A colorimeter, a DVDO AVLab 4K test pattern generator, and SpectraCal's CalMAN 5 software. After basic darkroom calibration (of which options are very limited in the Roku TV interface), we measured a black level of 0.07 cd/m2 and a peak brightness of 273.84 cd/m2 for a 3,912:1 contrast ratio. The Insignia NS-55DR420NA16, by comparison, displays a slightly darker 0.06 cd/m2 black level for a higher 4,453:1 contrast ratio. If you want significantly better performance than that, you should expect to spend at least twice as much for an HDTV with local backlight dimming (or nearly 80 times as much for the LG 55EG9600 and its curved OLED screen, which produces perfectly dark blacks and the best contrast we've seen on an HDTV).

While you can't do any sort of deep color calibration on the 40FS3800, its colors are thankfully very accurate out of the box. The chart above shows ideal color levels as boxes and measured color levels as dots, and all were very close to spot-on. This is impressive performance for a budget HDTV. The Insignia also shows very accurate colors, though, and it's a bit more vivid out of the box.

See How We Test HDTVs

You won't find very inky blacks on the 40FS3800, but shadow details were clear and dark scenes in Daredevil were well-balanced. The partially obscured, moody shots of alleys and warehouses were crisp and even, without shadows appearing washed-out or blacks looking pale. This isn't the darkest picture you can get, but for the price it's a solid performer.

Despite accurate colors in our tests, skin tones in The Big Lebowski appeared just slightly pale and undersaturated. The picture wasn't distractingly gray or skewed, but The Jesus' purple jumpsuit and the bowlers' faces weren't quite as vivid as they should have appeared. It's a relatively minor complaint, however, and saturation as a single setting can be adjusted fairly easily compared with a clear imbalance in red, green, or blue (which Roku TVs don't allow you to change individually at all).

Power and Input Lag
Input lag is the amount of time it takes between when an HDTV receives a signal and when the display updates. The 40FS3800 showed an input lag of 29.4 milliseconds, which is very solid for a budget HDTV. If you want to get much lower than that, you'll likely need to either get a much more expensive gaming monitor, or the (also much more expensive) Samsung JS9500 HDTV series, which holds the current record of just 1 ms of input lag.

Under normal viewing conditions, the 40FS3800 consumes 63 watts. The HDTV's Eco Save mode, which slightly dims the screen, drops that number to 51 watts. This is in line with other HDTVs of this size; the Hisense 40H4 uses a nearly identical 60 watts in calibrated Movie mode and 53 watts in Eco Save mode. 

Once again, TCL and Roku have combined to offer a functional and very affordable HDTV. The FS3800 series won't offer the deepest blacks or the most vibrant colors, but with the full selection of online services and apps through the Roku Channel Store, it's a solid choice if you want a connected screen for an affordable price. It doesn't distinguish itself against other Roku TVs, though; aside from different screen sizes available (40 and 50 inches, compared with 48 and 55 inches), the FS3800 seems identical to the FS3700 series. The Insignia NS-55DR420NA16 edges both models out in performance, though, and remains our Editors' Choice. But in this price range, any of the Roku TV models we've reviewed are worth considering.
