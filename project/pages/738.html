      Westworld: "The Bicameral Mind" Review

      Share.
      The Sweet Hereafter.
      By Eric Goldman

      Warning: Full spoilers for the episode below.

      The thing that made me nervous going into Westworld's season finale was that yes, at this point, William and the Man in Black being one and the same was blatantly obvious. So if the finale held off on this until the very end, saving it as a big “Gotcha!”, it would have been decidedly weak. That being the case, it was good to see that moment occur about half way through the episode, because now a weight had been lifted and we still had plenty of time left for other events to take precedence.

      Should the William/Man in Black reveal still have happened earlier in the season? Yes, I think so. It couldn’t help but feel like a bit of a waste of finale screen time seeing MIB explain it all to Dolores since most viewers could see it coming for so long, complete with William picking up a black hat for the first time. Still, the moment where he saw Dolores have the same “meet cute” with another guest that he’d had was impactful as far as showing his perception of her as someone meant for him shatter. Also, I was glad to at least have him tell her, “You never did escape,” since that was my big concern with this scenario since it was first suggested as a potential twist – that Dolores’ whole journey with William this season was actually a futile/pointless one, since she never did actually get away.

      So while it was true her first search for the maze didn't result in her freedom, it was at least acknowledged, and more importantly, we didn’t leave on that note. It sure doesn’t look like Dolores will be “rolled back” again now, and the reveal that the voice in her head was herself showed that she was now fully in control in a way she never had been. Plus, along the way, there was some satisfying take downs for William – viscerally, seeing Dolores kick his ass (arm breaking included!) and then simply the knowledge that the maze was something he truly couldn’t access and that he’d spent so much time invested in a futile hunt, while Dolores was going to finally truly evolve. That being said, I think it’s safe to assume that William survived that massacre at the end, or we would have seen a more definitive final moment for him than being shot in the arm.

      What was great about when he was shot though was his little grin, as here at last was the real danger he’d wanted from Westworld. Even though William is still a suitably hateable character, it was interesting to realize he and Ford essentially wanted the same thing by the end. Ford had come around to Arnold’s POV on the hosts deserving consciousness, and while William might not have been as high minded about it or want it for the same reasons, by wanting the hosts to have this freedom, he still wanted something very similar.

      It was very satisfying learning the full details on the massacre Teddy was involved in and that Dolores was Wyatt, thanks to Arnold’s tinkering. I have no idea if this was a common theory (I’d done my best to avoid reading too many theories being shared, though ones like MIB = William were everywhere), but it was a cool payoff that felt like it played fair with what we’d seen before and how poor Teddy was blending together different elements in his head.

      Meanwhile, Maeve’s big Westworld breakout was an intense, action-packed part of the finale, expertly directed by Jonathan Nolan (who co-wrote the episode with Lisa Joy) and delivering a ton of huge moments… First and foremost of which was SAMURAI WORLD! Yes, we got a little glimpse at another Delos theme park here (like the original Westworld movie included) and it was awesome.

      The entire sequence was captivating, as Hector and Armistice went full Terminator. Armistice’s joy at realizing what a modern gun to do was another memorable beat among many, and I’m glad we got that post-credit moment of her cutting her arm off to escape. In the midst of this though, it felt weird how dispassionate Felix was about the massacre happening around him. For Maeve, this could be seen as revenge, but wouldn’t Felix feel horrified to see his coworkers killed left and right or is he just that love-struck and amoral?

      Maeve going back to find her daughter at the end was amusingly both frustrating on a larger plot level (we’re still not going to see the outside world? And Maeve's whole breakout plan ended up not being needed thanks to a note given to her at the last minute?) yet also emotionally satisfying as far as showing Maeve was not simply coldhearted and that her memories of her daughter were too strong for her to ignore – especially after Bernard couldn’t just take them away.

      The finale once more showed what an amazing ensemble cast Westworld has. Anthony Hopkins conveying Ford’s melancholy final moments; Evan Rachel Woods playing out Dolores’ conversation with herself; Thandie Newton showing Maeve’s unstoppable resolve; Jeffrey Wright letting us see how Arnold’s sadness about his son motivated his final actions; that aforementioned smile from Ed Harris… There were just a ton of standout performances throughout, including the continuing strong work from Jimmi Simpson, James Marsden, Rodrigo Santoro and many more.

      Some bullet points to end on:

      -A disappointing lack of info on either Stubbs or Elsie in the finale, as we didn’t see either of them nor learn anything more about what happened to them. (yes, we now know Bernard choked Elsie, but we still didn’t see her die for sure, which feels like a red flag that she is alive)

      -What the hell happened to Logan? What was William trying to accomplish exactly sending him off naked on a horse? Security presumably wouldn’t have let him die, so beyond some embarrassment for Logan, what did this accomplish for William? (who apparently was indeed put in charge of Delos after this)

      -Okay, Maeve made it up that elevator, but it was still hard to believe the lobby wouldn’t be in lockdown at that point. Westworld security, you guys are not good. Maybe Stubbs would have done better if he’d been around!

      -The final scenes make it feel like Ford was a likely candidate to be the one who programmed Maeve to escape… in which point her choosing to go back instead for her daughter would be something he’d endorse, given he wanted the hosts to gain free will. But could someone else have programmed her?

      -What a great reveal that Dolores’ big death scene in Teddy’s arms was all being played out for the board. The scene was crossing the line into cheese and felt way too on the nose (“Maybe it’s just the beginning. The beginning of a brand new chapter”), so finding out it was all just part of the show was a very clever touch.

      -Felix pausing after learning the truth about Bernard, his vaguely robotic motions that followed, and Maeve exclaiming, “Oh, for f**k’s sake. You’re not one of us!” was a really funny beat in the middle of some really intense moments.

      The Verdict

      Westworld’s season finale wasn’t perfect, as it was hard to look past one notably obvious reveal coming later than it should have, while there were also some moments that felt a bit clunky or not fully explained. But overall, there was a lot to admire here, with several strong payoffs on what was building all season and big, bold events that signal things will be dramatically different for the series next season -- as the show's creators are indicating -- now that so much has changed.
