      The Walking Dead: How Negan and Carl's Relationship Compares to the Comics

      Share.
      Let's talk about the Judith situation.
      By Terri Schwartz

      Major spoilers for The Walking Dead, both TV show and comics, continue below. Read on at your own risk.

      Best laid plans, right? Carl's mission to infiltrate the Sanctuary and kill Negan was cut short almost immediately on The Walking Dead (despite his mowing down a couple Saviors along the way), but it did lead to some unexpected consequences. Instead of killing Carl upon his capture, Negan actually took the young Grimes under his wing for this week's extended episode, "Sing Me a Song," in a way that showed more depth of character in the Saviors' leader than we've seen so far.

      Click on the arrow to progress through and see how the TV show's depiction of Negan and Carl's relationship compares to the comics. Due to the graphic nature of both The Walking Dead comics and TV show, the slideshow below contains M-rated, violent imagery and strong language.

      The Carl and Negan storyline in The Walking Dead's Season 7 episode "Sing Me a Song" comes almost directly from the comics, with one key deviation at the end of the episode that means big differences for what comes next.

      The episode opens the same way in the TV show as Carl's arrival at the Sanctuary in the comics: he jumps out, machine gun blazing, trying to fulfill his goal of killing Negan.

      After Carl fires into Negan's Saviors trying to kill him upon his arrival to the Sanctuary, Negan in the comics seems a lot more defensive over Carl.

      Similarly, the comics depict Negan as a lot more physically imposing -- which is largely a bigger change across the board, as the comics' Negan is a bigger man than Jeffrey Dean Morgan's take on the character.

      Carl's reaction is pretty much the same between the two mediums: terrified, and totally confused.

      Advertisement

      Negan's capture of Carl happens pretty quickly in the comics, and it's quickly established just why Negan isn't killing his subordinate's rogue son.

      The Walking Dead show spends a bit more time establishing Negan's respect for Carl, because unlike Rick's son, it's pretty clear that the Saviors' leader admires Carl's wherewithal in coming to the Sanctuary.

      Negan's version of an ideal world with multiple wives is straight out of the comics, but it probably seems more idealized to a younger Carl.

      Negan's big power play over Carl is making him take the eye wrap off.

      And, just like in the comics, the power play works. Though Negan knows Carl's tough guy act is more than a front, he also knows that making him reveal his gaping eye socket is showcasing his vulnerability.

      Advertisement

      Maybe it's a different angle, but the reveal of Carl's eye has similar effect.

      The scene where Negan reacts to Carl revealing his destroyed eye socket plays out just about word-for-word like it does in the TV show.

      Jeffrey Dean Morgan plays Negan as surprised, and then apologetic when his power play actually plays out. This is one of the most pivotal moments in establishing what draws him to Carl, and why he is more sensitive with him than he is with other people.

      The comics present a similar depiction of a shocked Negan, though the same question remains: is he sincere, or is this just him manipulating Carl?

      "Sing Me a Song" is the name of the episode, and that too is a direct nod to the comics. Negan is a clearly unhinged character, and forces Carl to "sing him a song" as seeming payment for the men he killed.

      Advertisement

      Meanwhile, he highlights just how dangerous he is by swinging Lucille threateningly as Carl sings.

      The scene where Carl is singing and Negan is practicing his swing is a direct shot from the comics.

      Negan is a bit more sympathetic to Carl's death mother in the TV show, whereas here the comment comes off more like a threat. Since Lori died in a different way in the comics, his story about how he killed her seems to earn him some of Negan's respect.

      The dialogue from the show is taken almost straight out of the comics at multiple points, like here where Negan lectures the Saviors.

      Despite the divergent storylines in other parts of the episode, the majority of Negan and Carl's arc in "Sing Me a Song" comes straight from the source material, all the way down to most of the dialogue. It's interesting that the writing team decided to switch things up so much at the end, though. The most notable difference between the two versions of the story is that Judith is alive on the show while she's long dead in the comics. Seeing how that impacts Negan and Rick's inevitable confrontation will be very interesting to watch play out.
