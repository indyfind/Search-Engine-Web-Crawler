      The Walking Dead: "Sing Me a Song" Review

      Share.
      Mockin' the Suburbs.
      By Matt Fowler

      Warning: Full spoilers for the episode below.

      Many of the criticisms regarding this season's (over)use of Negan -- which started back in the season premiere and continued on in "Service" -- still hold firm here in "Sing Me a Song," a particularly Negan-heavy episode perched right before the midseason finale.

      Clocking in at an hour and a half, with commercials, "Sing Me a Song" took us back to the Saviors compound for, basically, a repeat tour. With Carl now as our new set of eyes -- er, eye -- we watched more snark and goofy trolling from Negan, more speeches about rules, more people being punished for breaking of said rules, more of Daryl getting pushed around, more of Dwight's knowing glances to Shelly - just more of most everything we've already experienced enough of this season.

      The main change-up here was Carl getting nabbed and Negan -- as the absolute epitome of a grandstanding, monologuing "big bad" who likes to spare adversaries because they amuse him with their spirit and fortitude -- trying to impress the revenge-driven teen by showing off his cool life of "luxuries." Negan was the leather jacket-clad "Uncle Jesse" this week, trying to woo Carl away from his grim life back in Alexandria with Rick and covert him. To Carl's credit though, despite being slightly caught off guard by Negan's harem of traumatized sex slaves, he resisted, and his story this week was probably the best storyline the boy's had on the show to date - outside of having to shoot his own mother.

      The Carl and Negan storyline in The Walking Dead's Season 7 episode "Sing Me a Song" comes almost directly from the comics, with one key deviation at the end of the episode that means big differences for what comes next.

      The episode opens the same way in the TV show as Carl's arrival at the Sanctuary in the comics: he jumps out, machine gun blazing, trying to fulfill his goal of killing Negan.

      After Carl fires into Negan's Saviors trying to kill him upon his arrival to the Sanctuary, Negan in the comics seems a lot more defensive over Carl.

      Similarly, the comics depict Negan as a lot more physically imposing -- which is largely a bigger change across the board, as the comics' Negan is a bigger man than Jeffrey Dean Morgan's take on the character.

      Carl's reaction is pretty much the same between the two mediums: terrified, and totally confused.

      Advertisement

      Negan's capture of Carl happens pretty quickly in the comics, and it's quickly established just why Negan isn't killing his subordinate's rogue son.

      The Walking Dead show spends a bit more time establishing Negan's respect for Carl, because unlike Rick's son, it's pretty clear that the Saviors' leader admires Carl's wherewithal in coming to the Sanctuary.

      Negan's version of an ideal world with multiple wives is straight out of the comics, but it probably seems more idealized to a younger Carl.

      Negan's big power play over Carl is making him take the eye wrap off.

      And, just like in the comics, the power play works. Though Negan knows Carl's tough guy act is more than a front, he also knows that making him reveal his gaping eye socket is showcasing his vulnerability.

      Advertisement

      Maybe it's a different angle, but the reveal of Carl's eye has similar effect.

      The scene where Negan reacts to Carl revealing his destroyed eye socket plays out just about word-for-word like it does in the TV show.

      Jeffrey Dean Morgan plays Negan as surprised, and then apologetic when his power play actually plays out. This is one of the most pivotal moments in establishing what draws him to Carl, and why he is more sensitive with him than he is with other people.

      The comics present a similar depiction of a shocked Negan, though the same question remains: is he sincere, or is this just him manipulating Carl?

      "Sing Me a Song" is the name of the episode, and that too is a direct nod to the comics. Negan is a clearly unhinged character, and forces Carl to "sing him a song" as seeming payment for the men he killed.

      Meanwhile, he highlights just how dangerous he is by swinging Lucille threateningly as Carl sings.

      The scene where Carl is singing and Negan is practicing his swing is a direct shot from the comics.

      Negan is a bit more sympathetic to Carl's death mother in the TV show, whereas here the comment comes off more like a threat. Since Lori died in a different way in the comics, his story about how he killed her seems to earn him some of Negan's respect.

      Since Lori died in a different way in the comics, his story about how he killed her seems to earn him some of Negan's respect.

      The dialogue from the show is taken almost straight out of the comics at multiple points, like here where Negan lectures the Saviors.

      Carl also got to gun down a couple Saviors on his way out of the truck and that was fairly badass. BUT, you have to wonder just how far Negan's fascination with Carl really goes. Really, Negan can lose two guys just like that and not physically punish Carl in some way? What will his men think of those lives going unavenged? Sure, they're all "Negan," but do they really appreciate being treated this disposably?

      If Negan falls in the midseason finale, he'll go down as one of the most flawed villains on the show. Carl even called him out for sparing the lives of people he probably shouldn't have. Of course, the names brought up were Rick and Daryl when, in fact, it's the women who are mostly trying to take Negan out. The question though now is: Will someone kill Negan simply because SO many people are out to kill him or will everyone's plan fail because they're not aligned? Sasha and Jesus have their own scheme going. Michonne's off on her own (we think), Rosita is in the same area as Negan now (and she has a bullet!) and Carl already blew his chance.

      Plus, you figure other communities that have axes to grind with Negan will have to play into any real form of effective retaliation - like Hilltop and the Kingdom.

      Carl learned a hard lesson here about trying to take matters into his own hands. No, not due to the slight psychological abuse he suffered when Negan decided to make him talk about past trauma and sing "You Are My Sunshine." No, Carl's actions, more or less, led Negan directly to Judith. Negan's now plopped on a porch, sipping lemonade, and making googly eyes at the girl. Will this be his bridge too far though? How will Rick react to this? He's submitting to Negan in order to protect the ones he loves, including Judith, so will Negan take the girl from him? Will that be the ultimate cost for Carl's mistake?

      "Sing Me A Song" certainly wasn't without its interesting moments - most specifically, some of the interactions between Negan and Carl (plus some pivotal Rosita/Eugene exchanges.) Again, this was Carl's best material in a long while and Negan's morbid curiosity with him is one of the most intriguing elements of the season. If anything, Negan's giving the show's B and C players some decent revenge arcs. Others are stepping up while Rick is shrinking back. Still though, this episode was also kind of an overlong mess. The show still seems to think, for whatever reason, that all-Negan-all-the-time is a good tactic. It's actually sort of a disservice to Jeffrey Dean Morgan's performance. The worst thing you want from this type of playful villain is for him to wear out his welcome, and Negan's been coasting on borrowed appreciation for weeks now.

      The Walking Dead sent two major characters into the lion's den in its latest episode, "Go Getters." While both Carl and Jesus's storylines mirror what happens in the comics, the way the TV show established their journeys sets up major differences down the road. Spoilers!

      Carl's departure was heavily foreshadowed, especially given his earlier confrontation with Negan. It took a little longer for the show to get to his decision to leave than the comics did, but both happened at around the right time.

      The big changes are how and why Jesus chose to head to the Saviors' camp as well. In the comics, he arrives in Sanctuary at the same time Carl does, but has no idea Rick's son is also there.

      In the comics, Carl's decision to leave comes the same day that Negan made his surprise visit to Alexandria.

      Instead of waiting or stewing, he sneaks into Negan's truck then and there and pitches a ride back to Sanctuary.

      Carl in the show needs a bit more instigation to get revenge on Negan. He ends up jumping into a truck at Hilltop that's driven by Saviors led by Simon (aka Trevor from GTA 5).

      Carl isn't purely driven by anger, either. He uses his feelings for Enid as an excuse to sneak out of Alexandria, but even she calls him on his BS and says he really wanted to get out for himself.

      It's clear that the real instigation for Carl's departure is his unhappiness with how Rick bent a knee to Negan in the last episode, "Service."

      In the comics, Jesus's quest for Sanctuary actually predates Negan's surprise visit to Alexandria. Rick ordered Jesus to follow after Dwight so he could try to find out where Negan lived, seemingly to plan an attack.

      Jesus ends Issue 102 tracking Dwight, and Issue 103 opens with him still on Dwight's tail. That's the same comics issue where Negan came to Alexandria, spurring Carl to run away from the safe zone and try to kill Negan himself.

      Advertisement

      In the show, it's Sasha who sends Jesus on his mission, not Rick. Considering Jesus is a major ally of Rick in the comics, it's easy to assume Sasha (and Maggie, to a lesser extent) will be his primary allies on the TV show -- for now.

      Though Jesus tells Sasha he is unhappy with not looping Maggie in on their plans, he sets out after Simon's Saviors to try to find out where Negan lives, seemingly so Sasha and Maggie can get their revenge for Negan killing Abraham and Glenn.

      Interestingly, the show already paid homage to the way Jesus tracked Dwight in the comics, even though he assumedly wasn't actually on Dwight's tail in the TV show.

      While Jesus is following Dwight in the comics, he fights off a walker on a bridge, and it falls to the highway where Dwight is walking, getting his attention and forcing Jesus to hide.

      Dwight comes across a similar walker-falling-off-a-bridge situation in episode 3, "The Cell," but it doesn't seem to be because of Jesus. Or has he been tracking the Saviors all along?

      Jesus's mission gets waylaid when the Saviors spot him and attack him.

      Instead of sneaking into the Saviors' compound, he gets knocked out and captured. By the time this happens, Carl has already arrived at Sanctuary.

      It's actually through Jesus's capture in Issue 104 that the comics audience gets their first look at Negan's home base.

      The show gave TV audiences their first look at Sanctuary through Daryl's capture back in episode 3, "The Cell."

      The big difference between the TV show and the comics is that, in the comics, Carl and Jesus are traveling to Sanctuary separately, not together. Considering the TV show is sending them there together, the outcome of their visit to Sanctuary will likely deviate significantly from the comics. Comics spoilers ahead!

      There was so much in this week's episode that could have been cut out. The burning of Mark with the hot iron. Whatever the hell's going on with Spencer. Negan's weird conversation with Olivia. Why did he all of sudden care that he hurt her feelings? The last time he was in Alexandria he was set to kill her, on the spot, for something that wasn't her fault. Sure, he could have been "playing nice" for the sake of Carl - but what good does that do after Carl already watched Negan bash Abraham and Glenn's skulls in?

      Elsewhere, Rick and Aaron found some sort of zombie-filled protection pond during their last-ditch scavenging efforts. The need to sneak some scenes into this week's chapter is understood since the next episode is the big fall finale, but why should we care about Rick finding something for Negan when we're also watching, ultimately, three or four different side stories involving characters trying to kill Negan? Is Rick going to find some huge prize? Maybe that's it. Rick uncovers some treasure that will appease Negan and send him back to the Sanctuary, all trespasses forgiven.

      More likely though, someone's gonna die next week. Rosita's plan seems destined to fail and it'll either cost her, or Eugene - or both. Eugene's cowardice being brought up again makes me feel like he's due for a hero moment, though with a character like Eugene that usually signals a final moment.

      The Verdict

      "Sing Me a Song" gave us handful of really good Negan/Carl scenes - ranging from hostile to docile to creepy. Negan has a way of communicating that simultaneously tears people down while also building them up. "Breaking each others' balls" is his preferred term for it, and it worked really well with Carl since no one's ever really treated him in a light-hearted manner. Yes, even though Negan's temperament is that of a smiling shark, his comments about Carl's disgusting-yet-rad eye socket seemed to empower Carl more than insult him.

      Still though, this episode traversed a lot of the same tire marks from earlier in the season. We learned a ton about Negan's factory back in "The Cell" so a lot of this week's episode felt like a rehash. Plus, having Negan in almost every scene dilutes his overall presence.
