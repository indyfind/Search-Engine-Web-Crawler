    • Beginner Tips for Writing

       Last Edit: January 18, 2013 - 3 years 10 months ago

    Writing is a weird thing. It's a skill we use almost every day, but creative or critical writing can be a daunting undertaking. You might look at something written by Colin Moriarty, Mitch Dyer, or Anthony Gallegos and think there's no way you can get to that level of writing.

    Not true, you can.

    Writing is an ever evolving skill and no one can truly master it, there's always something to learn. So now that we've covered that anyone can do it with just a little bit of practice, here's some tips to get you started.

    EditDon't like it? Don't write it.

    If you're going to write, you'll need a topic. Now, it's easy to sit down and think, "Oh, Colin Campbell wrote an awesome editorial about how gamer's are entitled. I'm not sure if I agree with that or if I even care, but it's what's popular this week. I'll write about it and hope to get noticed." But don't do that, as tempting as it is.

    Good writing can be done by anyone, but great writing comes from a place of passion. So pick something you love and write about it. It could be something broad that you feel you can provide a unique perspective on, or it can be an incredibly niche interest that you love and want to share with the world.

    EditTalk to yourself

    One of the biggest challenges writers face is conveying a thought that is crystal clear in their mind in a way that makes sense to others. Writing tends to happen in a stream of consciousness manner, and it's easy to write something and think, "Nailed it! Makes perfect sense." Well, yeah, the writer is definitely going to understand what he wrote because he wrote it.

    A good workaround for this, and something that is invaluable for burgeoning writers, is to read what is on the page aloud. Sounds crazy, yes, but by reading aloud long sentences will be highlighted, awkward phrasing will stick out, and (if the writing is done correctly) a narrative will be present.

    EditIt's all about perspective

    Time for a quick English lesson. There are three primary tenses/voices in the English language: first person, second person, and third person.

    First person refers to a piece of writing where the narrator is embodied within the piece. For instance, "I am writing a wiki page to help others who want to start blogs," is written in the first person perspective.

    Second person is when the narrator refers directly to the read, "You are reading this," is an example of second person narrative.

    Lastly, we have third person, "IGN readers who are curious about writing a blog are reading this page." It's a general rule that you want to keep the same "voice" throughout your writing, so try not to switch back and forth.

    If you're really serious and want people to genuinely enjoy your work, go here. It's an excellent source for grammar, style, and overall instruction on writing.

    EditAdd some style

    The all-powerful, possibly sentient machine known as Stephen Ng (he makes sure that the wikis don't explode) has some helpful code ready to insert into your IGN blog to give it some style. Check it out.

    Presentation is part of a successful blog. If you've seen a wall of text, you'd probably go back on your browser instead of reading it.

    EditRead ... a lot

    Athletes study their sport, doctors study medicine, writers study the words of others. If you're not reading, you're not challenging yourself with new ideas and different writing styles.

    This doesn't mean you have to go pick up some Tolstoy or other classic literature (although you might want to because a ton of it is amazing), but you need to be reading if you want to be writing.

    EditPractice

    Practice makes perfect, and in most cases, over-doing won't hurt. For blogging about hobbies, it's 50% journal entry and perhaps 50% handy tips ... and 100% effort.

    As you write or blog more, you will learn some topics are worth your time to pursue, and others not so much. In doing so, you may come to understand yourself more.

    EditHave fun

    Lastly, and most importantly, have fun. Writing can be an amazing escape and is incredibly fulfilling. When you write you're making something out of nothing, and that feeling is hard to top. So let loose, don't take yourself too seriously, and just write.

    Here's hoping this small page of tips is the first step in your prolific debut to the MyIGN blogging community.
