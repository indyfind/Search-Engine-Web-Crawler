      I had to vote Final Fantasy VII. It's presentation blew my mind when it came out, I remember my older brother's shocked reaction. It was a spectacle.

      And I think I appreciate it more as I get older. Yeah, by modern standards it's a bit clunky in the writing department, and the translation doesn't help there -- but both its script and its translation at the time were stellar.

      I've been replaying this and two things I've appreciated are the pacing and the sense of moral ambiguity it tries to create. Pacing-wise, something happens at pretty much every turn. The biggest lulls in the game precede Weapons tearing shit up. For the moral ambiguity, this is a game that opens with the heroes committing an act of terrorism. They get called out for killing innocents and for most of the game they don't give a shit. It's not until the end they admit that for the most part their quest to save the world has been secondary to their dreams of getting revenge. Could it be done better? Probably, but at the same time it's not like too many mainstream RPGs were operating in shades of gray before this.

      Gotta admit that FF7's graphics have aged rather badly. Playing it again this summer, it looked nothing like I remembered. It's still pretty for PS1 title, but, you know, not as pretty as I remembered.
      Click to expand...
      Eh, I think this is a shit critique that is often leveled at it. Yeah, it hasn't aged well. But it's Square's first major foray into 3D gaming on a radically different platform than what they've been working on. And it doesn't get credit for some the ways its graphics are used creatively. Pre-rendered backgrounds that aren't static are nifty, but touches like having elevators be FMV.

      Out of curiousity, I once looked at old reviews for FF7. The graphical concerns we have just weren't on the radar at the time. Because this was new technology that people were still learning to use. It's amazing how many of the games released in 1996 and 1997 were still 2D. Some genres embraced 3D a bit more willingly, but it seemed like RPGs were slower to change. I'd suggest that the JRPG world didn't really get behind Square's vision until the PS2 -- the SNES-style presentation was still really common even with later RPGs.
