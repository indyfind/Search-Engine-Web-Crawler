    Dec 7th - The Lost GuardianDecember 7, 2016 by ttdog666

    I was, and will ever remain to be, a massive fan of the game Ico.

    A game that has once again jumped to prominence because, after years and years of waiting, the third game from the development studio has finally released…

    … and it’s utterly, utterly… average.

    Had it released 5 or 6 years ago, thus only having spent 4 years in development, then I’d probably have bought a PlayStation 3 just for that game… but now… no rush… I can wait for a big sale.

    What passed for great game mechanics 10 years ago won’t cut it now… and while I can easily spend hours and hours grinding out levels in Skyrim to hit the required level to have a legendary Dragon appear so I can kill it… the idea of spending 10 minutes trying to get some dumb AI creature the size of a small house move into the correct spot does not appeal.

    The out-dated graphics I can handle… I grew up playing games when pixel art was the norm, not because the developers were going for the hipster retro look… but because that was a good as graphics got in those days… some of the machines I played games on couldn’t handle more than a couple of colours being too close together without the screen becoming a vibrant mess as we experienced what was known as “Colour Clash” on the ZX Spectrum.

    Maybe that’s why I detest and despise games with pixel art graphics that release now… they may be good games, but we’ve moved on, considerably, since the days of pixel art… consoles are way more powerful than the old ones could even dream of being… so why should I accept pixel-fucking-art graphics now?

    I can handle pretty poor levels of audio because at the same time that very same machine’s basic command for making sound was “BEEP” and was restricted to a single channel, even the more powerful Commodore 64 sounded like a weird techno beats machine in play and most games of that era had soundtracks written by some of the most awesome gaming musicians ever heard.

    I can handle a terrible story… or even no story at all, as long as it doesn’t divert to going up its own back passage with in jokes and pointless references… I’ve played some titles with hideous stories while I’ve passed over some great stories because, and this is the crucial thing here…

    … I demand quality gameplay.

    I passed over Uncharted 3 because the first two games were so bloody linear I wasn’t playing an adventure, I was playing a poorly disguised third person shooter and, with a lack of character progression, they quickly became the sort of game that I would happily ignore form here to eternity.

    Even when Uncharted ceased with the constant pew-pew of gun fights, you knew that one was just around the corner… for example there is a wonderful platforming section in two as you scale the three arms of a huge structure that will, when completed, reveal a map to the hidden treasure… the platforming is sublime, top notch stuff, but on the way to the room where this takes place the scenery is so obviously set for the gun fight as you try and leave… you know that once this section is complete that you’ll be running back down this corridor shooting away just to get out and be pushed towards the next set piece fight.

    I want gameplay above everything else in a game… not the promise of frustration and annoyance as the AI struggles to keep up with me, or flat out refuses to do what I want because it’s using algorithms that were potentially written 8 years ago when what we expected from an AI was far, far less than what we expect now.

    Add in some horrific stories, and YouTube videos of some horrendous slow down and it’s just not happening… apologists will babble on about there being slowdown in Shadow of the Colossus… but that was 10 years ago, when you see some of the visual feasts out there now, handling far better graphics and far more action, than we have here and it’s just inexcusable.

    The Last Guardian has gone from being the sort of game that would have made me buy a whole new console to play it… to being the sort of game I might pick up in a sale if the price is right.

    Shame.

    EDIT: Seems a good friend of mine has bought the game… so I guess I’ll be able to hear first hand about how it plays and what shortcomings it may have.

    Review - TorenDecember 5, 2016 by ttdog666

    There have been numerous instances these last couple of years where I’ve watched the E3 conferences and, among the mish-mash of pixelated Indie games (and I hate pixel graphics having been forced to grow up playing games on home computers like the Commodore 64 where they were the only option) usually shown as part of a 3 or 4 minute montage where 20 games are shown in a flurry… a game has really stood out to me.

    Games that appear to be trying something different, or stand out graphically, that I find myself inexplicably drawn to.

    A lot will fall by the wayside, Superhot looked interesting until I discovered it was a first person shooter… No Mans Sky was a must buy had we not had to wait close to 3 years since it was first shown and, as we’ve seen now it has been released, we have been presented with a mess of a game missing features we were promised all along and has proven to be as deep as a puddle… Cuphead which seems to have really nailed the 1920’s cartoon style but is in a genre I have zero interest in.

    Most others tend to release to a plethora of mediocre reviews… 5’s and 6’s (if they get that high) across the board… but I feel that I still want to play them.

    So, in the last few years I’ve played through Beyond Eyes, Submerged, Never Alone and Lifeless Planet and (for the most part) enjoyed them and feel that I’ve got my monies worth… now one of these games has landed on the PlayStation 4 and, while I ignored it before, found its way into one of the increasingly common Flash Sales and onto my hard drive… Toren.

    Story: This is a circle of life story… you awake as a child at the base of a tower, as you explore and “grow” through meditation and dreaming (which take the form of simple platforming sections) you climb the tree and learn more of its story and that of a great mage who seemed to succumb to power and now takes the form of a dragon (or something very similar) who you have to defeat.

    Gameplay: It’s very simple, walk, jump, swing the sword (once you get it) and that’s about it… some sections require you to light lamps to keep warm, others to trace out symbols to unlock the power or mystical memory to advance the story, and there are a handful of boss battles which lead up to the final assault at the top of the tower.

    Pros: It’s a passable story and there are some half decent ideas… from the logos at the start of the game it seems the Brazilian Culture Department had a hand in the game, even if only funding it’s development, so it probably has a more cultural significance to Brazilian gamers.

    The puzzles, when they appear, are not too taxing at all, with several paths that can be taken through some dream areas, and even if you miss these (I missed the very first dream section) you can still complete the game with little difficulty.

    It’s not a long game, which actually works in its favour… because, being honest, it’s not a very good game and clocking in at around 2-3 hours is all you’d really want to put into it.

    Cons: It looks rough as hell… the jumping animation is horrible… and there are some sections where any camera control is removed entirely so you have little idea what is ahead of you.

    Enemies seem entirely pointless, especially the small spiky ones which crawl along the floor, as they don’t seem to do anything if they get you while the other variety (yep… there are only 2 types of enemy) kill you instantly… there are crabs which walk around in one dream section but I have no idea if these do anything or are just decoration.

    Chapter Select, which is available once the game has been completed, seems backwards with the first level offered to replay being the last one and you work backwards to the start.

    Overall: It was a pleasant enough evening of entertainment… but I can’t see myself ever going back or even recommending it to people... what good ideas it has are crushed with some stunningly bad visuals and horrendous camera control (when you are allowed to control it) hampering what seems to be a passable story.

    It’s never a good sign in my mind when there is a Trophy/Achievement for being killed.

    Ratings: Graphics: A truly fugly game, and not stylised ugly as has been seen in Virginia… just flat out ugly graphics… 2/10

    Gameplay: Wonky camera hampers a few areas, but not enough variety in enemy (being 2 and the boss) and not enough variety in game play knocks it down several pegs… even as the meditation dreams go on you’re still just doing the same things as before only in a slightly different setting… 4/10

    Story: There is probably a decent story in there trying to emerge from the duff graphics and dodgy gameplay… possibly even a culturally significant one (as we’ve seen with Never Alone) but it’s just being choked by everything else… 6/10

    Overall: Pretty duff really… I don’t regret spending the money on it… but I’m glad I got it in a sale… 5/10

    Review - VirginiaDecember 2, 2016 by ttdog666

    I must have missed the meeting where gamers decided that what they really wanted to do in a game is to go for a big long walk whilst doing absolutely sod all.

    I’ve played too many games where you just wander around doing sweet f**k all, occasionally triggering dialogue where you’re told of the wonderful events that happened here a while ago… but you obviously missed out on… shame, seeing some of the actions described would have been fun.

    Virginia has been described in some places as the “best walking simulator yet” which is sort of like saying somebody is the “cleverest idiot” or “most beautiful person at ugly club” if you get my meaning.

    But, despite my mocking of the genre, I’ve had a couple of decent experiences with them… Dear Esther was mildly interesting, and I played Everybody’s Gone to the Rapture repeatedly to unlock all the Trophies and see all of the story… I even solved a few mysteries in The Vanishing of Ethan Carter… and now I find myself on Virginia… picked up cheap in a sale just before Black Friday.

    Story: You are a newly qualified FBI agent who is given the task of not only working a case with a colleague, but also investigating them as well… you go from scene to scene, often with large jumps/cuts forward in time to cut out some of the slow paced walking.

    The case you’re working is a missing child… as the case progresses and you find more information a more sinister undertone reveals itself.

    Gameplay: You walk around, the small reticule that marks where you’re looking is a dot which becomes a bigger circle when you see something you can interact with, this becomes a diamond shape when you are close enough to interact.

    There is no dialogue at all… everything is open to your own interpretation… the wobble of the camera as you walk is pretty sickening, I’ve played a few FPS games in the past and never come close to motion sickness or a heahache… here I suffered with both.

    Pros: It tells a passable story… and it has its own unique look and feel… but it could have been so much better.

    Cons: The lack of spoken dialogue handicaps this considerably… especially when the graphics are as stylised as this and the end sequences as so very stylised that you can’t always read facial expressions.

    It feels like the ending was rushed and there are times when your partner really ought to have spotted the file you have on her in your bag way before it actually happens… add in some truly hideously numbered Achievement totals and I’m struggling to think how I would have handled it had I not been able to complete the game fully and get back to a rounded Gamerscore total.

    Overall: I like the idea… but not the execution… had the graphics been more realistic rather the lack of dialogue may not have been an issue, but if you can’t hear the inflection of someone’s voice to get a handle on how they really feel, you can try and fathom it out from their body language… which doesn’t happen here.

    The collectables seem rather forced (there are two lots of 10 items) and don’t add anything… and the ending is way too vague and open to interpretation… which may work with a game like Inside but damages a game with as little gameplay as we have here.

    Ratings: Graphics: Very stylised and functional… but nothing more than that, with no voiced dialogue in the game you sometimes need extra clarity in what the game is showing you to make sure you understand… and here that just isn’t happening… 5/10

    Gameplay: Minimal… and the camera doesn’t help as I ended the game with both a headache and a nauseous feeling from the excessive wobble as you walk along… 4/10

    Story: The best part… but it runs out of steam after 60-65 minutes… and the whole final 15-20 ending sequence is too confusing… 5/10

    Overall: The idea seems sound, and the whole investigation angle works well… but its how they choose to tell the story that really damages the overall feel of the game… 5/10

    Nov 30th - Indiefest 2 - The CleansingNovember 30, 2016 by ttdog666

    The first “Indiefest” was an experiment… can I whore through a group of games in a weekend and have fun (if playing a couple of borderline garbage walking simulators can be described as fun) just for the points, and clear some of my rather large backlog at the same time?

    Sort of.

    Four games were completed in 4 days, the last of these (Firewatch) being the only one I’d recommend to other people and even then I’d throw in a warning that the ending sucked balls big time… but after four days play I’d added 4,000 Gamerscore and felt sated enough to go back to playing a game for fun.

    This Indiefest is different… these are being played out of curiosity mostly, one of them is heralded by some as an absolute classic, one I’ve talked about in the past has the potential to give me a spell of RSI by completing, and one is…well… another walking simulator.

    Reviews of all of these will land over the next week or so, depending on how long they take me and how much I pull my hair out playing them.

    Virginia Described in one review I’ve seen as the best walking simulator yet… which is sort of “damning with faint praise” sort of like saying you are the “most beautiful of all the ugly people” to someone.

    This one I have to complete for the full 1,000 else my OCD will kick in… all the Achievements have odd values, like 31 or 86… and I can’t handle it if my Gamerscore ends in anything other than 5 or 0.

    Toren The first game here I paid cash for, and the one that received the worst reviews of the lot… go figure.

    I’m not expecting much to be honest, platforming and fighting aren’t generally my favourite genre to explore but it was cheap and I bit and now it’ll have its day in the sun.

    Journey This was free on PS+ but by then I’d already bought a couple when it appeared in one of the many sales that the game has landed in over the last couple of years… a walking simulator of great repute this one I may watch a guide or two of just to make sure I don’t miss anything that pushes the game to reach it’s epic status.

    My Name Is Mayo The bastard child of games… and one which I’ve saved till the end to try and elevate the next big game I play (which will be Bioshock Infinite) above the mediocre level I currently hold it in… well… that was the plan.

    In the end it was played first and all over with after around 45 minutes… and my god (which is, incidentally, the flying spaghetti monster) was it appalling… hot garbage of the highest order… the pay off after 10,000 taps is… the lid comes off… whoop, fucking whoop!

    I’m not even going to give it the prestige of a review… suffice to say that even at $0.99 you’ll feel ripped off… 1/10 as it’s one of the worst things I have ever played… I feel dirty for having done so.

    Had a decent game landed in the Black Friday sale, or even in the November Flash Sale which dropped a day later, then this may have been dropped from the list, but as I intend to stick with the four I’ve selected and the first will have already been started by the time I post this on IGN, any future smaller titles I end up will be added to Indiefest 4’s line up.

    Review - Bioshock 2November 29, 2016 by ttdog666

    Now I guess I’m in a minority here… but I quite liked Bioshock 2.

    Sure it wasn’t up to the high standard story wise as the original game, but it did seem to play a little better than the original, they had clearly worked on a few of the issues some people had with the way the first game controlled… in much the same way that the second Knights of the Old Republic improved on the originals handling of grenades and shields… but story wise it was never going to compete.

    I, however, had a great time with this, walking around different areas of Rapture, slowly putting together your own back story, was fun… and I love the underwater setting way better than the cloud nonsense of Infinite… I have been looking forward to replaying this hidden gem of a title.

    Plus, and this is a massive plus, it had some top quality DLC in the shape of “Minerva’s Den” where the story came close to matching that of the original game… and there’s a huge part of me tempted to play that before I embark on my assault of the main campaign.

    Story: Something rotten is happening in Rapture… big surprise… and it’s your job to work out what… the who is known from the beginning.

    Having rescued all the Little Sisters from the original game someone is creating more of them, that someone is Sofia Lamb and the place needs a saviour to save it… you… one of the very first Big Daddies, who happened to be permanently linked to a Little Sister… Eleanor Lamb… spotted the connection?

    At various points in the game there are flash backs to before… before Sofia came… including the one where, under hypnosis, she had you remove your helmet and put a gun to your own head before pulling the trigger… time to find Eleanor and save Rapture.

    Gameplay: Being a Big Daddy from the start changes a few key things from the original game… whereas before to collect Adam you just defeated the Big Daddy and then rescued the Little Sister, here you have to first kill the Big Daddy, then protect the Little Sister as she harvests Adam from a corpse and draws the attention of the Splicers… then, once all this is done for every Little Sister in the level and you’ve rescued them… you have to deal with a “Big Sister” who is tougher than a Big Daddy and considerably quicker.

    Attack wise you can now effectively dual wield with both Plasmids and Weapons usable without having to switch between them in combat… which is a good thing, but early doors the weapons feel under-powered which isn’t… even in the early levels a boosted rivet gun still takes several shots to finish off a Splicer totally… even having researched them with the new version of the camera they can still prove to be a challenge.

    At least there are a few new enemies… the Brute Splicer has an air of “Goliath” about them from Borderlands 2 and these are certainly difficult to take down… but there are times when the game feels like it’s trying to make up for the less engaging story by just throwing enemies at you to kill in the hope that you won’t notice.

    Pros: Now this has to be a positive worth mentioning… Minerva’s Den comes as part of the package… and it’s some of the best DLC (with a proper, not blatantly obvious) twist, and even though I know what that is as I work my way through it… I don’t care.

    Minerva’s Den, despite having been cut off from other areas of Rapture for a considerable amount of time, still retains some of the quirks of the second game… deal with all the Little Sisters and a Big Sister will appear, and she’s harder to deal with because your weapons are (mostly) chronically underpowered… which makes everything a little more scary.

    It may not have the depth or size of the main game, but it’s a wonderful addition that I’m sort of glad I waited until I’d finished the main game to play.

    Back to the main game, the actual Bioshock 2 product, it’s fine… the solid gameplay of the original still works and while it is missing a boss battle at the end (and who can blame them for dropping it after the boss battle in the original) the ending I saw, from rescuing all the Little Sisters, is something I didn’t mind at all.

    Cons: Now it may just be me… but Bioshock 2 feels a lot more linear than the first game… a very set path which you have to follow which makes using a collectable guide for the audio logs very easy as you’ll always know which one you should find next… but takes away a little from the overall feel of the game.

    There is one element of Minerva’s Den that puzzles me… and annoys me no end… there are several doors which are locked by huge magnetic locks on the doors… no problem with that… except how you open them… you get a plasmid which creates a sort of black hole and the circuits which supply the locks with power are ripped free so the lock falls.

    All well and good… only you fire this plasmid over the top of the door through a conveniently placed gap between the top of the door wall and the ceiling… so any person, especially a spider splicer that can crawl on the ceiling, could surely climb through the gap into the room you’ve taken the time to put a huge lock on.

    Overall: Gameplay wise I sort of prefer Bioshock 2 to the original game… it plays much smoother, but the story isn’t on the same level… there is no gigantic twist to the story and while the ending misses the climax of a boss battle, running around the final corner to what you believe to be safety, only to find a massive pile of explosives, is certainly unexpected.

    What has struck me since I completed the main story line is that I don’t ever remember playing this game through and not saving all the Little Sisters, not sparing Grace, Stanley and Gil… not once have I been a ruthless, vicious bastard and slaughtered everybody who gets in my way.

    Maybe over the Christmas break.

    Ratings: Graphics: Not having played the original for a while (or really any other AAA shooter recently) Iit’s hard to say if these really compare to what we are presented with in the genre these days… but they look sharp enough that you won’t feel like you’re playing a game of from five years ago… 7/10

    Gameplay: Solid, doesn’t try to change what wasn’t broken from the first game, but refines it a touch and smooths out the odd rough edge from the original… 8/10

    Story: Not up to the biblical (by which I mean a fantasy story of the highest quality) levels of the first game, but by no means a stinker either… 7/10

    Overall: I adore the Bioshock name, 1 and 2 are of the highest quality and there is clearly scope within the Rapture universe for more stories in other areas of the vast underwater world… but I doubt we’ll ever see them… shame… 8/10
