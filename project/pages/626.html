      A Beginner’s Guide to Hearthstone

      Share.
      17 important tips designed to help newer players.
      By Cam Shea

      Hearthstone is an intimidatingly deep game: it’s multi-faceted and strategic, there’s a lot to learn and there’s also a big card pool you don’t have access to out of the gate. It’s the sort of game that takes months to really come to grips with, and even then you’ve still got an enormous amount to learn.

      There are, however, some fundamental pieces of advice that will help accelerate that learning process, and that’s what this feature is about. It’s primarily aimed at new-ish players and those that have been playing for a while but aren’t making much headway on the ladder.

      To be extra clear: This isn’t a guide for people who have yet to play Hearthstone at all. I’m assuming you’ve played through the tutorial, unlocked all the heroes, and know the basics—at the very least. The game’s tutorial and its practice mode do a good job of introducing the mechanics, as does Blizzard’s own Hearthstone site – How to Play, Hero overviews, Lessons and tips for new players.

      Every single player has his or her own level of knowledge, experience and capability, but hopefully there’ll be plenty in what follows for you to muse on, and help you improve! Don't forget to also check out our A to Z of Hearthstone for an alphabetical trip through key cards from Blizzard's CCG.

      1) Card knowledge lets you accelerate your learning

      This is obvious, but it’s not just the core mechanics you have to learn in Hearthstone—it’s also the cards. Being able to recognize cards at a glance in your hand, and know what the stats are and what they do opens so many doors to further learning. You don’t need to have an encyclopedic knowledge of every card in the game, either—the goal early on should be to know the core cards that make up most of the common deck archetypes, as well as some fringe/tech choices that pop up regularly.

      The best way to get that knowledge is by playing, playing and playing some more. When your opponent does something you don’t quite understand look through the play history and read the card text, then put the interactions together in your head. We’ll talk about what you should be playing in a bit, but the point here is that playing a lot—against actual humans—will quickly give you a grounding in the key cards, which then allows you to start enjoying the strategy of the game as a spectator.

      2) Watch tournaments

      Seeing how the best players in the world play Hearthstone will teach you an enormous amount. This point follows the one above because you’ll need basic card knowledge to get much out of tournament play. Even if the casters are doing a good job catering to newer players when discussing the plays and options, there’s still invariably assumed knowledge. Watching tournaments will also clue you in to what the most popular decks look like (albeit tweaked tournament versions) and how they interact with one another.

      You can either tune in to live tournaments on Twitch (Blizzard’s channel, ESL, etc.) or catch the VODs on YouTube later (Blizzard’s channel, ESL, etc.). The Competitive Hearthstone subreddit is a good place to keep tabs on the tournament scene.

      3) Find streamers you like and can learn from

      Now that your card and game knowledge has hit a good level it’s time to immerse yourself in a different kind of learning, and that’s via Twitch and YouTube. Hearthstone is an ideal game for streaming services like Twitch thanks to the cadence of the gameplay—there’s time to talk through what’s going on in detail, not to mention time to engage with chat and foster a strong sense of community.

      There are dozens of great Hearthstone streams out there, and each has something a little different to offer. Some are about entertainment first and foremost – playing fun decks and experimenting, while others are geared towards ladder performance. There are plenty that are very much focused on helping you get better too. They’re the players that are talking through every play in full and answering questions from chat around specific plays, cards and so on.

      “

      ...try and make your own play before the player does and see how they compare. What did the pro take into account that you didn’t?

      When you’re watching a stream—or Hearthstone play in general—you should also try and get into the habit of watching actively to try and make your own play before the player does and see how they compare. What did the pro take into account that you didn’t?

      If you don’t have the time to tune into live gameplay, plenty of streamers post highlight clips to YouTube. There are also, obviously, people making content specifically for YouTube, so you really need to be paying attention to both sites.

      To get you started on Twitch, check out StrifeCro (great player and deckbuilder who gives plenty of insight into his plays), Brian Kibler (well-known Magic pro who also talks through his plays concisely), Kripparrian (one of the biggest channels out there -  he plays a mix of fun and homebrewed competitive decks and a mix of constructed and Arena), Amaz (one of the most theatrical players there – owns Team Archon) and Day9 (old school streamer with an infectious personality).

      Just about every big name has a channel though, so, if you like, say Savjz’s style, look him up! Other names to check out: Trump, Firebat, Thijs, Reynad, Noxious and Kolento. Most of these players have YouTube channels too, but the one I’d really highlight is Kripparrian’s because he does bespoke daily videos that combine on-point analysis of different aspects of Hearthstone with clips highlighting the topic.

      If you’re interested in getting better at Arena there’s a whole community for that mode too. Look up ADWCTA, Merps, Hafu, Ratsmah and Shadybunny. These guys—and gal—basically ONLY play Arena.

      4) The Hearthstone community is huge, so use it!

      There’s no end to the resources available for Hearthstone players online. There’s the Hearthstone subreddit, the Competitive Hearthstone subreddit and the Arena subreddit, not to mention a heap of community sites that let you do things like build and share decklists.

      Oh, and there are plenty of great Hearthstone podcasts out there too. A few to get you started: The Angry Chicken, Value Town, Top Deck Kings, Coin Concede and Well Met! And again, if you’re after Arena, The Lightforge (hosted by ADWCTA and Merps) focuses on nothing but Arena and is incredibly insightful.

      5) Find a deck you like and learn it inside and out

      It’s one thing to have a general understanding of how a deck plays, and quite another to know it inside and out. Instead of flitting about between decks, pick one you like and get really good at it. Learning the ins and outs of a deck takes time, but getting the mulligan right, knowing what you’re looking for and working towards setting up your win condition is really satisfying. It can make a significant difference to your overall win percentage too.

      A Zoolock decklist.

      How to choose that deck? Well, ideally you want to choose something that has a play style you enjoy, but if you’re new to the game, for instance, and want to play Control Warrior, well, you’re out of luck because that archetype is expensive. My advice would be to take a look at a couple of meta reports (try Tempo Storm and Vicious Syndicate) and find a strong deck that you can build – or at least build a variant of with its core cards intact.

      A good, affordable starter deck is something like Zoolock—a type of Warlock that floods the board with minions, buffs them to make favourable trades (i.e. using a one drop to kill a three drop, or killing a minion while keeping yours alive) and snowballs based on board control and the ability to quickly pump minions out. Aggro Shaman is also affordable. (And by that I mean that it’s largely comprised of common and rare cards, and not that many epic and legendary cards.) Both of those decks are very competitive too.

      If you’re really short on cards, look around for budget decklists. A player named Sheng, for instance, put together budget decks for all nine classes after the Whispers of the Old Gods expansion came out earlier this year. That might be a good place to start if you’re not planning on buying the newest adventure - One Night in Karazhan.

      Knowing your deck thoroughly will then help you with the next point.

      6) Analyse what you’re facing

      A big part of piloting your deck well is understanding what your opponent is trying to do. There are a lot of archetypes to learn right now, so it’s going to take time, but the more consciously you think about it the better. Some decks can be recognised within a turn or two, others may take a little longer, and even then you’ll never really be 100% sure of the cards they’re running. Nevertheless, you should be playing based on what you think you’re facing.

      “

      The five main terms you’ll hear are aggro, tempo, midrange, control and combo.

      It’s probably worth quickly going over some of the terms that get used in reference to broad deck archetypes in Hearthstone. The five main terms you’ll hear are aggro, tempo, midrange, control and combo.

      Aggro decks try to rush you down as quickly as possible. They do minimal minion trading and simply try to maximise face (i.e. hero) damage to kill you before you can do anything to stop them. These decks have very low mana curves (the cost of the cards included) to help ensure they can get off to a fast start every single game, and essentially put you on a clock. If you can weather the storm and potentially even heal or taunt up they’ll often run out of gas if the game goes on long enough. Face Hunter was the aggro deck to beat at one point, but is no longer viable, while the current king, Aggro Shaman, relies more on minion trading in the early game to get a board lead, then bursts you down.

      Some of the key Aggro Shaman cards. This archetype can have an explosive start.

      Tempo decks are all about using your mana as efficiently as possible to get an advantage on the board, allowing you to make favourable trades/remove their minions and put your opponent permanently on the back foot. Tempo Mage, for instance, uses minions like Mana Wyrm, Sorcerer’s Apprentice, Cult Sorcerer and Flamewaker to get additional value out of cheap spells that help it control the board in the early game. Zoolock—while very aggressive and fast—is also a tempo deck. It locks down the board in the early game using minions before switching to face damage in the mid game.

      These minions come into their own when used with the many spells Tempo Mage runs.

      Midrange decks typically have a higher mana curve than tempo decks, and look to play strong minions on curve (i.e. play a two drop on two, a three drop on three, etc.) and attempt to out-value their opponent to gain an advantage in the early to mid-game that can then be capitalised on to close the game out before it goes too long. Midrange Hunter, for instance, has a strong early game, plenty of removal tools and a hard-to-beat mid-late game thanks to Savannah Highmane and Call of the Wild.

      So many strong minions and spells. Call of the Wild is a disgusting card.

      Control decks have weak early games in terms of minions and instead rely on being able to stall their opponents to get to a strong late game. Control Warrior, for instance, has lots of ways to remove an opponent’s minions and keep the board clear early on then gain the advantage with bigger threats as the game progresses.

      Control Paladin (which is currently built around N’Zoth) uses minions like Doomsayer to either prevent face damage or keep the board clear, as well as minions like Aldor Peacekeeper and Keeper of Uldaman to reduce damage or negate big threats. It also has powerful board clears like Wild Pyromancer + Equality, and Equality + Consecration, not to mention the healing to help sustain it to the late game, at which point it drops threats like Cairne, Sylvanas, Ragnaros (sometimes both varieties) and Tirion Fordring, before resurrecting a bunch of them with N’Zoth.

      Bringing minions like this back is ridiculous. Of course, you have to get to the late game first...

      Combo decks require multiple cards to pull off their win condition. An example would be Patron Warrior, which needs the tools to create as many patrons as possible on a single explosive turn, or OTK Worgen Warrior, which can burst an enemy down from full health given the right cards in hand. Combo decks have a lot of card-draw to ensure players can draw through their deck to find their combo pieces before they get killed.

      Powerful.

      Of course, none of these archetypes are walled off from the others, so a midrange deck can scale all the way from faster, more aggressive builds through to much more controlling. Similarly, some decks have combo elements that can function as a win condition, but it’s not their only win condition. Malygos Druid, for instance, might be able to burst you down using Malygos, but it can also win through board control and big minions. Hunter, also, used to be divided into Face Hunter and Midrange Hunter, but 2015 saw the development of Hybrid Hunter, which fused the two archetypes.

      Knowing the style of deck your opponent is playing—even if you don’t know all the cards—is obviously going to inform the decisions you make. If you’re playing midrange against a more aggressive deck, for instance, you’re on the defensive, trying to shut them down for long enough to turn the game around. If you’re midrange against a control deck, you really have to be the aggressor and try to end the game before their big threats can destroy you.

      So they’re the broad archetypes, but the next step is understanding the shells (the cards that make up the core) for specific decks. This lets you anticipate what the opponent will try to do and thus attempt to play around their best-case plays or save removal for a strong turn you know is coming. You can also proactively try and bait things out – get your opponent to waste resources dealing with a sacrificial minion in order to play something more powerful that will stick.

      Crucial Priest removal.

      An example of knowing an opponent’s deck and game plan would be if you were playing Control Priest against a Control Paladin running N’Zoth. The game is going to go long, because that’s just the way this match-up plays out, so you know you have to extract every ounce of value out of your cards. Of particular importance are the two Entombs in your deck. If you can hit Sylvanas and Tirion Fordring with them not only do you take those insanely powerful minions away from your opponent and shuffle them into your own deck, but you’ve also prevented them from coming back when your opponent plays N’Zoth. Thus, in this match-up you’ll be very very reluctant to use those two cards to deal with anything else.

      This is the sort of stuff that—as your knowledge grows—you should try to be cognisant of, so that you can avoid being out-played wherever possible. It will also help you with the next point.
