      REZ Infinite’s New Area Explodes on PS VR with Sound and Immersion

      Share.
      Rez is back, and its new area might be one of PlayStation VR’s best experiences.
      By Jose Otero

      Just mere minutes into a PlayStation VR demo of Rez Infinite’s new stage, called Area X, and we’re spellbound. The new area coming to PlayStation this October as part of a remastered Rez experience is a masterclass of evocative trance music, beautifully timed transitions, and gorgeous particle effects. It’s not particularly challenging, but Rez has never had to be.

      You can play without VR, but Sony’s headset gives Rez a level of immersion it has never had before and it’s hard to go back to the plain old TV once you’ve experienced it.

      1 Minute of Rez Infinite's Breathtaking New Area X
      01:04
      Got feedback on our player?
      We want to hear it.

      Up until this demo, Rez was best described as an on-rails shooter where trance music, cyberpunk, and video games came together to form a breathtaking digital experience. While most of that is still true, Area X rewrites the 15 year-old playbook. The result is an experience that trades in pre-set paths and sharp vector graphics for wide open freedom and explosive effects.

      After a brief tutorial, Area X begins with you alone in a black room. The team behind this new space decided to make some changes for this experience. The on-rails portion is gone, and the player avatar can now move forward, backwards, and fly around to chase down targets. Once you get a handle on the controls, a small ball of white light appears in front of you. It materialises as the sound of a sharp digital horn is heard in the distance.

      The single glowing ball floats back and forth surrounded by four small light trails that crisscross around it. It hangs in the air almost daring you to lock on and take a shot. We did, and the first hit activates what sounds like a single cymbal that echoes in the room that breaks the ambient background noise. We follow up with a volley of seven lock-ons and it begins. You audibly hear the familiar Rez countdown as a digital voice ticks down in rapid succession: “7, 6, 5, 4, 3, 2…, 1!”

      Rez infinite: A Trip Down Memory Lane
      01:00
      Got feedback on our player?
      We want to hear it.

      Seven rays of light shoot out from the center of the ball, as the energy in the room is building up. The lights partially illuminates the space like a lone firework and suddenly dawns on us we’re in a very large area. The light explodes as all the digital noises in the room build to a crescendo, and BOOM! Your avatar zooms forward into the first of several areas as the pulsing techno soundtrack begins with an area full of bell chimes.

      What follows is roughly 15-20 minutes of head-bobbin’ digital melody that builds up beautifully, with sound samples added into the mix every time you shoot an enemy target down. Each downed foe explodes into a shower of particle patterns that almost feel like fireworks. And that’s just the first few minutes of the experience. We don’t want to spoil the jaw-dropping things we saw later on.

      Rez’s new content makes its spiritual successor Child of Eden and its colorfully rich environments a memory. The new area is Mizuguchi and his team doing the follow-up to Rez that fans always wanted. It’s a trance music deep dive that left us breathless.

      It’s important to note that you can play Rez Infinite without VR and still walk away with a top-notch experience. In fact, Infinite presents Rez at its very best. 1080p 60fps (we learned Rez HD on Xbox 360 was limited to 720p). It also runs in 4K on the PlayStation 4 Pro, if you’re interested in playing it on the beefed-up PlayStation 4 when it launches in November.

      Rez Infinite: 1 Minutes of Area 2 Gameplay
      01:05
      Got feedback on our player?
      We want to hear it.

      But that’s not all that’s changed. The sound design has been remastered to support full 3D audio and 7.1 Surround. Even the menus have gotten some tweaks to make it VR friendly. It’s all part of a value play that presents Rez in its most technically superior state but also adds the option for virtual reality, a feature that arguably was the only thing missing when the original game launched in 2001.

      In 2008, Rez HD revived a classic game and gave producer Tetsuya Mizuguchi’s masterpiece a second life. In 2016, Rez Infinite ups the ante by adding the missing piece of the puzzle in PlayStation VR and a new playable area that will warm the heart of any Rez fan. There’s no denying Rez is a short game that’s infinitely replayable, but VR adds the final piece of the puzzle.

      When it was over, Rez Infinite’s Area X left us wanting to go back in again and again. We played it again on a 4K TV afterwards and it was good, but just not as unbelievable as the first time in VR. The IGN review for Rez HD from 2008 says that “Rez is the manifestation of every '80s or '90s pop culture take on the future of videogames, minus the virtual reality headset.“ Whelp, it’s time to scratch that last one off the list and embrace a bold new take on what Rez can be.

      Jose Otero is an Editor at IGN and host of Nintendo Voice Chat. You can follow him on Twitter.
