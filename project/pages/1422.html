    I’m Going to Go out on a LimbDecember 6, 2016 by jeriahking

     

    Thursday, after watching The Game Awards and letting it resonate with me; it quickly came to my realization that one of my favorite parts of it in its entirety wasn’t any title that was shown, but rather something else entirely. It wasn’t a game preview and it wasn’t a demo of a new title being brought to light, but rather something that was so well said. Something that I tend to forget as I partake in this form of media that I so dearly love. Something that doesn’t always translate easily while embarking in a game and letting yourself fully go while strapping on your headset or grabbing that controller. And the wording was so wonderfully crafted during Nolan North’s acceptance speech, regarding when he won for Uncharted 4.

    He had stated, “I’m going to go out on a limb. I want to thank a group of people at Naughty Dog. It’s them as a whole. I’m hearing a lot of talk lately about how performance matters. And it does. The performance of every designer, every programmer, every artist, every hard-working talented person at that office that performance is so important their performance matters more than mine. That’s important to understand in this day and age with all the talk going back and forth. Because without their performance, my performance would not only not matter, it wouldn’t exist.”

    It’s easy to always fall on one side of the issue and support voice actors side of the coin, and rightfully so in some cases, because it’s their job and some of the horrendous working conditions that have been floating around for some is insane. And in no sense am I making this post into a “voice actors strike is ludicrous” ordeal because I’m not anything close to representing the amazing talent of them. On the flip side of that coin, it’s too easy to forget the hard work that creators, programmers, concept artists, directors, and everyone inside that studio has gone through to make that piece of work. It’s simple to collapse under pressure if the game wasn’t as good as you thought it would be and say something negative about it. Taking into consideration that people have given years of their life away from their families, late at night, early in the morning, to do what they love and get that finished product into the community they cherish as well. It will always be relevant and extremely important to remember that they love video games as well as we do.

    Being related in the computer field and dealing with small steps of programming, it’s without any doubt tedious work. It’s grueling and unforgiving. One wrong move at the start and everything explodes. Granted they’re pros, so they’re going to be miles and miles better than myself, but I know how tough it can be. And that ties in back to Nolan’s words. Without their nights after nights of constantly going back to the drawing board, and perfecting the art, we wouldn’t even come anywhere close to seeing the amazing performances portrayed from the voice actors that represent and bring to life those characters.

    Do you feel that it was appropiate to give a speech like this? Do you feel the same way as I do?

    Walk Through the ValleyDecember 5, 2016 by jeriahking

    If you've been anywhere near the presence of the Internet within the last 3 days, there's a high possibility that you've seen the massive flow of excitement and some caution from one of my favorite games of all time. It's justifiable as to why there is so much happiness in the air however, it's also justifiable as to why there is so much nervousness as well since these characters and the story that the game crafts so beautifully is so close to many of our hearts. We’ve already gotten added updates about the game; such as Ellie will be the main character, the main theme of this game is hate, and that the composer of The Last of Us, Gustavo Santaolalla, will be back in this complementary half. Rather than dissect the trailer or talk about speculations, I want to delve into a little different territory. Within those three days since PSX, there was also a piece of notable information that stood out to. Bruce Straley, the director of The Last of Us will not be returning to the second half of this continuation.

    The Last of Us 2: PSX 2016 Reveal Trailer
    04:01
    Got feedback on our player?
    We want to hear it.

    Sources were floating around and the statement that was given was that Straley needed to take some time off since he put out two of the biggest critically acclaimed games in the PS3's lifetime. In no way, shape, or form am I anything close to representing a developer or a director for that matter so this very well may be the truth. We always hear how hard it is for game developers and studios to make games and rightfully so since they're giving months and years working on their title, sacrificing time away from their families. Being on the Internet when this information was released and seeing the worry that the game won't have the same feel and greatness to it that The Last of Us had, isn't necessarily the case. It's granted that some motifs of The Last of Us Part II won't be the same, and that should be viewed in a positive light since many don't want an exact cut and copy of the first title. Change is good to any title and this may very well be the change that is welcomed.

    Now is the time to put our trust in the hands of everyone at Naughty Dog. Now is the time to put our trust in the game's new co-director. Neil Druckmann, the creative director over at Naughty Dog that also helped mold The Last of Us 2 had said it best when he said something along the lines of that they love the characters too and they wouldn't continue this story if they felt there wasn't more to dive into. Since I don't think it will be released until 2019 earliest, all we can do now is wait. I for one, can't wait to dive back head first into that atmosphere and Naughty Dog's masterful hand at storytelling.

    Are you as excited about The Last of Us Part II as I am? What were your favorite moments from PSX?

    Diversity Done Right May 23, 2016 by JeriahKing

    Diversity within gaming is one of those words that are thrown around in just about any new title that is released. Diversity itself, however around us is what makes our world interesting. It’s what makes new foods intriguing, meeting new people, and visiting foreign places, more exciting than ever. With that said, in gaming, diversity means giving the gamer a character in game that they can in sorts, relate to.

    Blizzard Artist Turns Child's Overwatch Fan Art Into Concept Art
    01:18
    Got feedback on our player?
    We want to hear it.

    Diversity doesn’t and shouldn't ever mean just plopping a fictional character in game that isn't Caucasian. It should never be developers just meeting a sort of quota in their head. Rather, diversity in game translates into being proud of whoever and whatever race or background that you come from. Rather, it’s about gamers everywhere, being proud to play as whoever they want to, regardless of race, background, or gender. With that said, it’s easy for developers to say they don’t care about diversity, but with the Internet being so prodding, they’re less often to take the road less taken. That’s where the compliment comes in on Blizzard’s behalf.

    Let’s take a look at some facts to prove as to why I’m giving Blizzard’s, Overwatch a round of applause. Out of the 21 characters, seven of them are female. While this isn’t an insane amount of female leads, it’s still pretty solid for it being a first-person shooter. Even with that said, the females in the game range from many different kinds. For example, the contrast between Widowmaker and Zarya are incredibly different. With Widowmaker being more slim and wearing the tight fit outfit, Zarya actually looks like the female character that seems bad ass, that has the muscles that looks like she can wield the gun that she does, and that’s a big deal to me.

    Even racially, Overwatch nails where the bar should be set at. Rather than just mash characters from around the world in game; it plays into the story wonderfully since the game takes place inside of war torn universe. The character list is chalked up full of characters from every part of the world, including Japan, Nepal, India, Germany, and Brazil.

    Without a doubt, Overwatch, with it just barely being released, is already giving the term of diversity, the proper dignity it deservers. When the game itself is built from the ground up with the idea of implementing diversity not just for the sake of it itself, but because the developer wants the game to have it, the audience picks up on that. The Internet picks up on that; and that honesty and creativity is hard to criticize. If Overwatch does anywhere how it’s expected to, it’s success will show develops the proper way to implement one of the hardest topics in gaming.

    How do you feel about diversity in gaming? Do you think Blizzard is handling is as they should?

    Capcom’s Resident Redemption May 19, 2016 by JeriahKing

    Resident Evil has gotten far away from its original intended roots. What was intended to be a cohesive new take on the horror genre, ended up turning into a shoot off with zombies. With rumors of Resident Evil 7 being in the works, what can the series do the improve off of the last installment and take it back to the glorious days of my preferred Resident Evil game, 4?

    Horror games have a pretty close place to my heart. With that said, as much as horror has changed over the years, and as much as what feels like every aspect of what can be done in horror regarding games has been done. It’s hard for me to enjoy a horror game that actually feels tense and meshes that well with other essential gameplay mechanics. A huge part of this change plays into not many people being into the idea of playing a to the core survival horror game.  They would rather have this category mixed with a platformer or a first-person shooter. And those sales in turn, influence developers to turn to implementing different genres into the survival horror original framework. I believe this was the sole reason as to why Resident Evil, ever since after the fourth one, has taken a turn into the game playing largely around shooting your way out of everything, which kills the tension of the overall experience.

    For example, the game Outlast keeps popping up in my head. Simply because they nailed the feeling of the on the edge type of gameplay that the survival horror roots are supposed to have. There was always an uneasiness in the game due to the brilliant cam-corder function and needing to put batteries in it. Even though, that game crumbled massively towards the ending and lost its pace of what it had going in the beginning for myself, I definitely feel as if Red Barrels nailed the core feeling as to what survival horror is supposed to be like today.

    I would love to see a AAA production of what a good old school, Resident Evil would look like. One that is driven by an actual story, rather than a story that starts off slow as in terms of pacing, which is great, but then spirals out into something that Resident Evil always tends to do. And that thing being, always turning the game towards having you fight these huge monsters. I want a story that makes sense in terms of where you’re to stop the outbreak. A game that is so heavily intertwined in the story, but still always is backed up by great mechanics, and an actual feeling of tension.

    Do you think survival horror has waned during the years? What is your favorite survival horror game?
