      The Original Xbox’s 15 Best Exclusive Games

      Share.
      The OG Xbox is 15! These are its best games you couldn’t play anywhere else.
      By Ryan McCaffrey

      Fifteen years ago today, Microsoft released the original Xbox, a console whose legacy lives on today in the form of the Xbox One and Xbox Live. Microsoft’s first games machine – originally codenamed the DirectXbox – was practically a PC. But by virtue of its PC-like built-in 8GB hard drive and Ethernet adapter, along with its infamous not-so-PC-like first controller (the Duke!), it left its mark with a memorable game library during its all-too-brief four-year run. And while it saw many great games grace its DVD tray, these are our favorite 15 exclusives* to have ever hit the original Xbox.

      (*Some of these games came to other platforms much later, but were for all intents and purposes Xbox-exclusive at the time. Or their PS2/GameCube versions were just so inferior that you wouldn't want to play them on those platforms, like Splinter Cell Chaos Theory. Feel free to argue about it in the comments below.)

      15

      Steel Battalion

      Odds are you never played Steel Battalion. At $200, it cost almost as much as the Xbox itself. But we’re willing to bet that you’ve at least heard of its legend. Capcom’s Vertical Tank game was about as much of a simulation as a sci-fi giant-robot game could be, thanks to its bespoke 40-button controller and foot-pedal box. You had to go through a complicated series of precise switch throws and button presses just to start up your VT, and if you didn’t hit the controller’s eject button in time when you were critically damaged, your save game was wiped out and you had to start the entire campaign over. Due to its cost, complexity, and obviously limited sales potential, Steel Battalion would almost certainly never get greenlit today. Thank goodness Capcom made this a real thing in 2002.

      14

      Breakdown

      Another deep cut that’s near and dear to die-hard Xbox fans, Breakdown is an obscure Namco gem that’s secretly one of the best sci-fi games of its entire console generation. Breakdown was steadfastly first-person at all times, long before the likes of Mirror’s Edge or anything else. Its most famous scene saw you eat a poisoned hamburger and then almost immediately vomit it back up. Breakdown was barely a shooter. It was mostly a melee combat game, but since you developed Neo-in-the-Matrix-like superpowers over the course of the riveting story, the staunch first-person perspective only enhanced your connection to the gameplay (except when you actually did have to pick up a gun. Those controls...weren’t good). The boss battle with Solus at the end was one for the ages, and the power curve and fantastic sci-fi story made Breakdown a cult classic.

      13

      Jade Empire

      While BioWare’s Knights of the Old Republic team was busy dreaming up the game that would become Mass Effect, BioWare’s other developers released Jade Empire, an epic role-playing game set in the rich and largely-untapped-by-games setting of ancient China. Its combat, dialogue, and morality system largely followed KOTOR’s template. That’s a good thing, obviously. Meanwhile, Jade Empire’s sense of culture and BioWare’s excellent character development made for a memorable RPG that criminally few people ended up playing.

      12

      Psychonauts

      Legendary game designer Tim Schafer’s mind-walking platformer went through development hell, but lucky for us, it did finally see the light of day on Xbox in 2005. You played as Raz, a young camp-goer at Whispering Rock Psychic Summer Camp, a place that taught kids how to harness their psychic abilities. Smart platforming and clever puzzles melded into lastingly memorable missions (the Meat Circus!), which were complemented by Schafer’s usual sense of humor alongside gorgeous art direction. Hopefully more people will play the long-awaited sequel that’s finally in the works.

      11

      Full Spectrum Warrior

      This third-person simulation – you almost never pulled the trigger yourself – was originally commissioned by the US Army as a training tool for real-life soldiers. Later, the now-defunct developer Pandemic turned it into a full-fledged game. But there’s nothing “game-y” about Full Spectrum Warrior. It is a tactical strategy game through-and-through, played out in tense, stressful real-time. You must coordinate your A, B, and sometimes C fireteams at all times, not only putting them in positions to succeed but also keeping them from being put in harm’s way. Though the graphics were realistic but hardly lifelike, failure was nevertheless a haunting scenario – a chilling video game insight into the razor-thin, life-and-death margins that real-life soldiers faced during that time. (Full Spectrum Warrior was released during the height of the Iraq War.)

      10

      MechAssault

      MechAssault took the sim-leaning MechWarrior PC franchise – which was based on a pen-and-paper RPG – and transformed it into a fantastic, fast-paced robot romp, both in the single-player campaign and especially as one of the founding multiplayer games on Xbox Live. It turned out that when you took the familiar MechWarrior classes, stripped out all of the Mech management, and made it all about the action, it was really, really fun. Why this series didn’t survive is a head-scratcher.

      9

      Crimson Skies: High Road to Revenge

      Perhaps the cult-classic-est of Xbox cult classics, Crimson Skies, like MechAssault, took an established Microsoft PC IP and console-ified it in a smart and fun way. High Road to Revenge was a semi-open-world flight combat game where you could, at select locations, get out of your plane and hop into gun turrets – still a new concept back then. Its graphics were outstanding, and again it proved to be one of Xbox Live’s earliest multiplayer hits. It was a product of the now-defunct FASA Studio, which could explain why we never got a sequel despite a steady undercurrent of support for it among hardcore Xbox fans over the years.

      8

      Project Gotham Racing 2

      If you’ve played any of the incredible Forza Horizon games, know that they owe their existence to Project Gotham Racing. It was a visually stunning, engrossing racing game that pioneered the careful balancing act of simulation handling and physics with arcade-style racing fun that Horizon carries the mantle for now. The Kudos system, which awarded you points for driving stylishly, rewarded you for more than just crossing the finish line first. Coupled with an excellent suite of Xbox Live multiplayer options, it was a phenomenal racing game for players who wanted a realistic experience but didn’t necessarily want to worry about tweaking tire pressures and tuning suspension settings.
