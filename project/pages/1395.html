    Driving Passion in Games - Part 2: Catch-A-RideDecember 7, 2016 by andri_g

    Hello again, friends! This series might not fly, but, so far, it's been a smooth ride.

     

    I recently started playing Borderlands (because, backlog). The desolate landscape and run-down structures remind me of Fallout with two major exceptions. First, most of the doors in Borderlands' structures are welded shut, which I find both disappointing and annoying. Second, early in the game, you gain access to a vehicle: the Catch-A-Ride, for roaming the expansive outlands.

     

     

    Catch-A-Ride - Borderlands

    The Catch-A-Ride vehicle has split-input controls: the left stick is for acceleration, breaking, and reverse, and the right stick is for direction steering. The camera is in third-person view, outside of the vehicle, with its position control locked to the direction steering controls. While this does scheme does feel awkward at first, the camera does spring-back, re-positioning itself behind the Catch-A-Ride once your vehicle starts moving.

     

     

    The main drawback is that the Catch-A-Ride's turret weapon, either machine gun or rocket launcher (your choice), can only be used by parking the vehicle and having your character physically climb into the turret. The turret weapon is most useful when you need to thin-out a horde of weapon-wielding or vehicle driving enemies. Otherwise, you usually can rely on the vehicle's armor to withstand enemy projectiles and battering long enough to make them run for cover or be run-down in the process.

     

     

    While you do also gain access to fast-travel service later in the game, these stations usually are positioned at or close by the local Catch-A-Ride station. Meaning you're never very far from being able to call-up a vehicle to drive to the doorstep of your next destination.

     

    Note: The terrain in Borderlands is rough and what few strips of paved roads and beaten paths remain are short and hedged-in by enemy barricades and rakk dens. That's okay though; the Catch-A-Ride is built more like a baja racer than a street vehicle. So all you have to worry about is holding down the accelerator and leaving most of your troubles behind in a cloud of dust. Unless it gets so severely damaged in an enemy ambush that it explodes, but that's another story (you don't have to pay for a replacement rides, but you might not survive your walk to the nearest Catch-A-Ride station).

     

    Have you driven the Catch-A-Ride? If so, what did you think of it?

    Driving Passion in Games - Part 1: The MakoDecember 5, 2016 by andri_g

    Hello again, friends!

     

    Since MyIGN has decided to let me post *a* blog again, I will take advantage of the opportunity. So write, think, and be witty, friends, for tomorrow... we may be denied (?)!

     

    Open-world games are great. Open-world games with vehicles are even better. This is especially true in games where you have the most resilient, if not only, vehicle in service. It adds a host of game-play possibilities to your gaming experience. Here are my three most memorable open-world game vehicles.

     

    Mako - Mass Effect

    The controls for the Mako is like a toy RC vehicle. Steering and movement is positioned as if you were in the driver's seat. The steering is in the direction of the controller, as if you are inside and looking out through the "windshield". The camera is in third-person view, outside of the Mako, with movement and positioning that's completely independent of the vehicle itself.

     

     

    In case you haven't driven the Mako (yet), the disconnect comes when the camera has you looking at the vehicle from any position except behind. You still have to adjust your steering as if you were sitting in the driver's seat and facing forward. If the camera drifts or the Mako hits an obstacle, causing it to change direction relative to the camera, you must adjust your steering, speed, and the camera itself in order to make sure you're still heading towards the destination you set out for. The Mako does have underside thrusters. These can be used to hop over gaps while moving forward or to slow the vehicle's descent after accidentally driving off of a cliff--a welcome feature, indeed.

     

    For the most part, the Mako is an exploration vehicle. It has relatively weak shields and armor which can be easily damaged or destroyed. If this happens, you must pay to repair or replace it. This makes trying to use the Mako to run-down targets, other than the weakest or most exposed enemies in the game, risky and potentially very expensive.

     

     

    Added to this, you must toggle the camera controls to put you in first-person view of the Mako's cannon to aim it, possibly while you are driving the Mako, full-speed ahead (or reverse). This control scheme is tank controls plus-plus, and adds-up to Mass Effect's one most noticeable and note-worthy minus.

     

    Have you driven the Mako before? If so, what did you think of it?

    "Cheated" (?) by Modern Game CompaniesNovember 27, 2016 by andri_g

    Hello again, friends!

     

    After reading clinta13's entitlement blog, I was inspired to examine the flip-side of gamer's sense of entitlement--namely, gamers cheated out of stuff promised to entice a buy. I look at how publishers disenfranchise gamers by failing to give gamers what gamers have come to expect: things previously offered, accepted and, therefore, owed, but not delivered.

     

    More out of a sense of fair use than personal entitlement, I set the scenario for this topic in a re-purposed scene from the original Splinter Cell game**. Doing so makes this more fun and interesting to read about while still highlighting the uneven relationship power-dynamic in negotiations and exchanges between individual gamers and game companies. Enjoy!

     

    ** Scene transcript (original): http://splintercell.wikia.com/wiki/Kalinatek/Transcript

     

    -

    (DevPub pitches a game promised to kill boredom and interest gamers.)

    DevPub: Are you a gamer?

    Gamer: I.. Yes. You are developer? You have game?

    DevPub: Yes and No. The game is on its way. Until it gets here, I am the only distraction you got. And I am not a very good one.

    Gamer: We have to play. We'll have to hurry, boredom will find us soon.

    DevPub: That's not my job. I am here for your money.

    Gamer: That wasn't the deal.

    DevPub: The deal still stands. The devs will get you out of here, entertained, but first you have to give me the money.

    Gamer: That wasn't the deal I accepted from the information on the game pre-order page.

    DevPub: Listen, do I come to your parents and tell them to tell you how to buy a game?

    Gamer: What?!

    DevPub: No. So don't come to my job and tell me how to do mine. The dev's game is out of beta and on the way. You are going to be fine. You can give me the money or I can take it.

    Gamer: That wasn't the deal.

    DevPub: You're working from a very limited phrasebook. Listen, just give me the money. I'm tired and hate making gamers scream. It gets other gamers down.

    Gamer: Here. *Hands DevPub the money.*

    DevPub: Thanks, and until the dev's *finished* game is released, you'd better find another way to entertain yourself.

    Gamer: God dammit!
