    My Top 5 Announcements from Playstation Experience 2016December 5, 2016 by jr8zeppelin

     

    5. Wipeout Omega Collection

    I have never fully indulged in a Wipeout game before. Sure, it may be because I was never too big into racers; I played Need for Speed and Mario Kart as a kid, but that was about the extent of it. I did give Wipeout 2048, a launch game for the Playstation Vita, a few tries, but I've always felt as if racing games work better on consoles than anything else. Therefore, I am actually pumped for this collection. 2048 will be included in the three-part collection along with 2008's Wipeout HD, as well its expansion, Wipeout Fury HD. We aren't sure when exactly this collection is set to release, but the team is aiming for sometime over the upcoming summer.

    4. PS4 Remasters

    About half way through the conference, Shuehei announced that three classic games are being remastered for the Playstation 4. Shu stated that the remasters will feature native 4K support on PS4 Pro and "other improvements as well". I am excited for these three remasters for the exact reason I'm pumped for the previous three: I have never played PaRappa, LocoRoco or Patapon. I know, I know. What kind of Playstation fan am I? Well, I'm a newfound one. At least, I'm a newfound hardcore fan. I've always played titles from Playstation such as Spyro, Jak and Ratchet, but I never even heard of PaRappa when I was growing up. As for the PSP exclusives, LocoRoco and Patapon; I did have a PSP growing up, though I was unfamiliar with these games, as well. That is, until I started listening to Greg Miller's podcasts a few years back and just HAD to do some research after hearing his many rants about Patapon. I'm very excited to try all three of these classic Playstation titles for the first time.

    3. Nex Machina

    Housemarque, an independent studio based in Finland, most prominently recognized for creating the Stardust series, is one of the most underrated and talented studios in the industry. They're a team that just understands how to make an appealing game with incredibly fun controls, exuberant visuals and a remarkable sense of addiction. They're the king of modern twin-stick shooters. Nex Machina is a top-down shooter, and they recently released one of those earlier this year in the form of Alienation. For that reason, I must admit, I am far more thrilled for Housemarque's previously announced title; Matterfall.Â Don't get me wrong, though; I am still immensely excited for Nex Machina, as it is being made in collaboration with Eugene Jarvis, and the team described it as their "most explosive top-down shooter yet" and a "true arcade experience". There is no word yet as to when this title will see the light of day, but I'd say it is safe to assume that it will not release in 2017.

    2. Uncharted: The Lost Legacy

    Uncharted is my second favorite gaming franchise to-date. Its most recent installment, Uncharted 4: A Thief's End, was no "The Last of Us", but it was one of the most impressive video games of all time, from a graphics, gameplay performance and narrative perspective. It was near-perfect on every level. I did have one solitary complaint regarding the campaign, though: there was no Chloe Frazer. This daring and charismatic Australian was introduced in Uncharted 2: Among Thieves and reprised her role in Uncharted 3: Drake's Deception, but was unfortunately absent from the fourth entry. We knew going into the Playstation Experience showcase that Uncharted 4 would be getting single-player DLC, but many fans speculated that Sam Drake would be the playable character. I greatly prefer Chloe, as we have never controlled a woman in this series before and her personality is amazing. We also got some meaty details on the expansion:

    Nadine Ross, a female character introduced in A Thief's End, will be joining Chloe and her pre-Uncharted 4 story. The campaign will be shorter than Uncharted 4's, but lengthier than The Last of Us' "Left Behind" DLC. We even know that The Lost Legacy will feature open areas larger than those in Uncharted 4, driving mechanics will possibly return, and Chloe's fighting style is far different from Drake's. There is no word yet as to when this expansion will drop to PS4 players, but I'd say sometime later next year is a pretty safe bet.

    1. The Last of Us: Part II

    Here it is, ladies and gentlemen! The sequel to one of the most well-directed and highest-rated video games of all time! The Last of Us: Part II takes place five years after the events of The Last of Us, as Ellie is now 19 years old and was a meer 14 when she was gunning down clickers and poking holes in dudes in the first game. Neil Druckman stated that "Ellie is the star" and that "this story is about hate", two things that were heavily hinted at in the reveal trailer.

    The Last of Us was a worldwide phenomenon when released, and is still looked upon as one of the best games of all time. It holds a very special place in my heart like no other, because it was released on June 14, 2013, my seventeenth birthday, meaning it was the first Mature-rated game I ever legally bought. Which isn't saying much, but picking up my Post-Pandemic Edition (featuring the large statue of Joel and Ellie) and playing the flawless campaign all day and night is one of my fondest birthday memories. Naughty Dog is a master at their craft, creating a desolate-yet-rich world, a tremendous cast of appealing characters, a terrifying array of enemies and a captivatingly emotional story that absolutely seduces every type of gamer.

    You really can't end a conference in a more memorable and integral fashion than with a sequel to The Last of Us. The game won dozens upon dozens of awards in 2013, is still selling like hotcakes to this day with the PS4's remastered version, and remains my second favorite video game of all-time. The Last of Us: Part II is undoubtedly my most anticipated video game, and I eagerly await further release information.

    Thank you, Naughty Dog, for constantly pleasing my heart and soul with your work. Your video games, from Jak and Daxter: The Precursor Legacy (my all time favorite game) to Uncharted 2: Among Thieves, speak to me on a different level than anything else I have ever experienced. You are the greatest, from this day, until your last.

    Monthly Gaming Recap - September October 2, 2016 by jr8zeppelin

     

    Dust: An Elysian Tail

    This game, which released in 2012 for PC and Xbox Live Arcade back in 2012, was ported to Sony's Playstation 4 in 2014. I played the game for the first time that year and absolutely loved the play through. I was relatively blown away, actually. It's a Metroidvania game that combines 2D side-scrolling elements with hack n' slash fighting mechanics, as well as RPG and puzzle elements. Dust features an enormous amount of well-written dialogue that feels fresh and exclusive to this majestic fantasy world, a phenomenal combat system overwhelmed with charm, astounding graphics with breathtaking landscapes, and a great amount of side quests, each telling their own story that perfectly fits in conjunction with the overarching plot. The side characters in this low-budget title provided some of the most entertaining interactions and dramatic voyages in any indie title to date. I started my second play through of this masterful video game in September, and after obtaining the platinum trophy, I longed for more quests to complete and treasures to unlock. Thanks to one of my all-time favorite combat systems, truly memorable characters and stunning environments, dreaming of a sequel to Dust: An Elysian Tail just won't cut it any longer; I need more Metroidvania goodness!

    Verdict = 10/10

    Rocket League Rumble

    As many of you may know, if you have happened to tune in to my page over the last year, that I simply cannot stop playing Rocket League. I indulge in a few matches (almost) every night and have been writing about its greatness since it released on PS4 July of last year. Well, now, Psyonix has released a free new update called Rumble, which adds thirteen terrific power-ups to the already-chaotic soccer matches. One of my favorites, for instance, are the spikes, which attach the ball to your car whenever you touch it, allowing you to carry it into the goal as long as you avoid enemy cars. Another top-quality power-up is the grappling hook, one that hooks the ball and pulls you straight to it at high speeds. There are also the more lackluster abilities like "the boot" and "the swapper", which kick your opponents and sends them launching and swaps them and yourself with each of your locations, respectively. This update does not just spice up the gameplay; it's sort of a completely new game in and of itself. Rocket League was already one of the most inventive experiences in video games, but Rumble somehow expanded that experience in a brilliantly enjoyable manner.

    Life is Strange

    Adventure games are special to me, as they are so magnificently different from most other video games, and they're a terrific change of pace to the countless number of gameplay governed titles. Some gamers knock the Adventure genre, dubbing the games as "Walking Simulators", but dammit, most of them are downright entertaining and offer tons of strenuous choices to make throughout the story, which I find to be thoroughly enjoyable, as they leave me playing on the edge of my seat with anticipation and excitement. Life is Strange, published by Square Enix and made by Dontnod Entertainment, might just be the most well made and original game in the genre. Not only does the game feature a large number of absolutely arduous decisions that the player must make, but these choices seem to have far more meaningful consequences than other decision-based adventure games. And did I mention the playable character is a superhero who can bend time? This allows the player to use time manipulation in order to solve a unique variety of puzzles, and even rewind to change answers after regretting your decision on a quick-time-event. Life is Strange is one of the most inventive adventure games to date, featuring a phenomenal roster of characters, an amazing gameplay mechanic in the form of time-manipulation, and the best graphics that the genre has ever seen.

    Verdict = 9.5Â 

    Super Smash Bros Wii U

    Again, if any of you have followed my page before and are familiar with my reborn monthly gaming recaps, you will know that Super Smash Bros for the Wii U is one of my favorite games of all time. Ever since Melee, I've been one of the most prominent smash bros players in my city, playing in and winning a few tournaments and making several friends all because of this beloved franchise. Well, three of my best friends have recently moved in together, so I've been keeping my Wii U at their house, because they have people over and play it far more often than I do by myself (as you can tell, I'm more of a Playstation guy). This game offers unlimited entertainment throughout its many game modes, although we get all of the pleasure we need by competing in simple Smash matches, whether it be with two people, four, or even eight. Super Smash Bros Wii U is, in my honest opinion, the best game released in the past three years, and Nintendo has supported it brilliantly with a great number of downloadable characters and stages.

    Verdict = 10/10

    All Birddogs go to Heaven - The Last Guardian PreviewAugust 10, 2016 by jr8zeppelin

    Genre: Action/Adventure

    Developer: genDESIGN

    Publisher: Sony Interactive Entertainment

    Platforms: PS4

    Release Date: December 6, 2016

    Development

    The Last Guardian's development cycle is one of the most curiously fascinating tales in video game history. It is the third game by legendary game designer Fumito Ueda, proceeding ICO and Shadow of the Colossus, two Playstation 2 exclusives that are amongst the highest rated video games ever made. The Last Guardian was announced and showcased during Sony's 2009 E3 press conference, a whopping seven years ago, and has been in development since two years prior to the announcement. With a projected release of 2011 on the Playstation 3, the game suffered numerous heartbreaking delays in the coming years due to technical issues.

    It was recently announced by the President of Sony's Worldwide Studios, Shuhei Yoshida, that the reveal trailer for The Last Guardian was "specced up", and the game actually ran at a much lower framerate on the PS3. Team ICO, a rather small team in comparison to other Sony studios, had trouble keeping up with Ueda's vision for the Playstation 3 hardware. In 2011, after Sony's decision to delay the game, Ueda and other Team ICO members chose to leave Sony and create a new studio called genDESIGN, but stayed on under contract to continue work on The Last Guardian. Ueda continued as game director, leaving Sony's own Studio Japan to handle the programming and technical specs. It was decided, in 2012, to push the development to the then upcoming Playstation 4 system.

    Years went by with no news or updates on the game's state, with many fans and industry insiders assuming the game was dead and gone. Then, in 2015, Sony opened up their impressive E3 press conference by re-revealing The Last Guardian, running smooth and sound on the Playstation 4. The core gameplay mechanics have not altered from the original PlayStation 3 version to the PlayStation 4, but with the more-powerful system, the team was able to put more detail into the characters and the environment.

    The long-awaited release date was revealed earlier this year at Sony's E3 conference as October 25, 2016. But, of course, it endured another delay as Sony pushed the release date a few weeks back to December 6. Alas, there is nothing more to fear! That was the game's final delay, as it has now gone gold and is almost ready to deliver!

    Story

    The story is stylized as a narrative flashback. The narrator, an older man, reflects his childhood encounters with a humongous, feathered creature named Trico. This truly galvanizing beast has been designed as an amalgamation of several different animals, with Ueda stating it will "create a greater sense of empathy in the player". The Last Guardian is, more or less, a telling of the augmenting relationship between the boy and the creature. It's a unusual bond that develops wonderfully as the two force their way through a mysterious, guarded castle by means of cooperation, trust and dedication. Ueda stated that the ultimate goal of the game is to "escape from this environment somehow, and that's for the player to experience as the story unfolds."

    Based on the small amount of gameplay we have seen, it seems as if the animations and movements of the characters will play a large role in our inordinate love for them. The boy moves somewhat clumsily and with a lack of balance, adding a great sense of self-devotion to the player. Trico, on the other hand, is a rather timid creature who often seems startled by quick movement. The team has put an imposing amount of time into these characters, and I am sure we players will love them undeniably.

    The beginning of the game has been shown to the public, so those who chose to indulge in the gameplay video are sure to have a nice understanding as to just what this game is, but I'm to refrain from any spoilers in this thread, obviously, so just know that this game will feature a gripping, heart-rending storyline, much like ICO and Shadow of the Colossus, and will most likely make you feel a slew of mixed emotions by the end of the campaign.

    Gameplay

    The Last Guardian is played in the third-person perspective and, much like ICO, combines action-adventure and puzzle mechanics. The player controls the young boy, who is initially weaponless, forcing him to use his surroundings to sneak past or strategically defeat shadow-being guards who are posted all around castle. These guards are somewhat slow and lackadaisical, but are strategically placed to make equitable levels. The guards can capture the boy, and if the player is incapable of freeing him within a certain period of time, the game will end.

    The game combines elements and is very reminiscent of both ICO and Shadow of the Colossus. With Shadow of the Colossus, the main focus of the game was the dynamic relationship with the giant creatures, which is where Trico's size plays in. ICO was, more or less, centered around the cooperation between Yorda and the boy. The only difference here is that Yorda could be directly controlled; Trico, however, must be directed by the boy, not commanded by the press of a button.

    The boy is weak but harbors a logical, strategic mind - the mind of the player. It is up to the boy to utilize Trico's size and strength in order to gain entry into each forthcoming area by luring the creature with food, climbing him to reach tall ledges or structures, using him as a raft to cross water, etcâ€¦ but the boy must also solve puzzles on his own from time to time without the help of his giant, feathery friend.

    It is Actually Happening!

    It has been eleven years since this team has released a game, meaning they skipped out on an entire generation of consoles. Video gamers everywhere have contemplated for nearly a decade as to whether or not this game would ever see the light of day, but now, with the game just around the corner, our fears have been diminished. The Last Guardian is real, and it drops on the Playstation 4 tomorrow! It currently has a fantastic score of 82 on Metacritic, with critics raving over the rollercoaster-of-emotions that is the bond between the boy and trico. Some sites are complaining about the wonky controls and fidgety camera, but it doesn't seem to break the overall experience. I'll leave some trailers/demos below, for those of you wishing to check out this long-awaited title. Thanks for the read!

    Jr8zeppelin's GOTY WatchAugust 3, 2016 by jr8zeppelin

    2016, so far, has been a tremendous year for video games, with huge titles releasing in the first half of the year that speak to every kind of gamer. Fighting games, puzzle games, action games, adventure games, platformers, and, most notably, first-person shooters have all seen huge releases in 2016, and we still have five months left in the year. I have played 15 games that have come out so far this year, and only four of them are potential "Game of the Year" contenders.

    4. The Witness

    I was eagerly awaiting the other three games on this list for months, hell, even years before they released. I never expected myself to purchase an open-world puzzle game of The Witness's caliber, but after critics raved and rambled on and on about it days before launch, I decided to pick it up day one. That was a tremendous decision. It is technically and indie game, but the large scope of The Witness's world, the game's breathtaking visuals, and its astounding attention to detail in terms of gameplay was truly something special. Something about those simple-looking puzzles was, in one word, addicting, leaving a remarkable sense of accomplishment after completing just one (out of 600) of the puzzles. The moments that were truly special were due to the game forcing the player to use his or her surroundings to solve certain puzzles. I never thought I would enjoy using the environment to solve a simple puzzle, but the puzzles featured in The Witness are not at all simple, and they made tremendous use of memorization and awareness. This game really tested my limits as a gamer and I will never forget the colossal amount of hours that I put into it.

    3. Alienation

    Alienation was announced way back in 2014, which some might consider to be a long time for an indie game, but the wait was absolutely, 100% worth it. Finnish developer Housemarque is, in my honest opinion, the most underrated studio in the industry. They consistently put out amazing and fun-to-play titles like Super Stardust, Resogun, Dead Nation and now this, and all of them have tremendous amounts of replay value. Alienation is Housemarque's biggest title to date, and it's also their prettiest and most technically impressive. With three character classes to choose from (a tank, a saboteur and a bio-specialist), numerous amounts of intimidating enemies and a fabulous loot/upgrade system, Housemarque really went over-the-top in creating the biggest and most addicting top-down shooter to date. Each class looks, feels, and plays different from one another, and working with teammates in cooperative online mode has never felt so smooth and functional. Alienation will undoubtedly be a candidate for my favorite game of 2016, though it will likely lose out to other, specific, powerhouses...

    2. Ratchet and Clank

    This is a game that I knew would be great from the minute Insomniac announced it as a Playstation 4 exclusive, but I was never too big on the Ratchet and Clank franchise growing up, so this game's eminence really snuck up on me. I'm a huge platformer gamer, so I knew I would have a fun time, but it wasn't because of the platforming that bring Ratchet and Clank this high up on my list, it was everything; the eye-candy, pixar-like graphics, the vast array of terrifically unique and enjoyable weapons, the entertaining story, precise shooting mechanics and excellent traversals are just a few reasons as to why this game deserves a play through from gamers everywhere, no matter what your taste. Surprisingly, this game quickly flew the ranks and is now one of my favorite games ever made, which has me IMMENSELY excited that Insomniac announced they'll be the ones bringing my second favorite superhero of all time to the Playstation 4. I simply cannot wait for more information on the Spider-Man game... It holds such potential for Sony, and I have a grand feeling that Insomniac won't screw it up.

    1. Uncharted 4: A Thief's End

    I just raved and ranted about three titles as if they are among the industry's best, but that title still belongs to the Naughty Gods. The talented team, who also brought us the likes of Crash Bandicoot, Jak and Daxter, and the highly praised The Last of Us, delivered yet another masterpiece with Uncharted 4, the supposed final installment in one of the best and most iconic franchises in the gaming industry. Uncharted 4 brought us more of the same thrilling action sequences that we know and love, from intense car chases to strenuous wall climbing, but what really stands out about this iteration is the refined gameplay. The shooting controls have improved drastically, stellar stealth mechanics now exist (similar to those featured in franchises like Assassin's Creed and Metal Gear Solid), and the campaign is far less linear than previous entries. I would still say that Uncharted 2: Among Thieves is my favorite entry overall, thanks to an impressive cast of lovable characters, including an immensely intimidating villain, and also because of the setting. Don't get me wrong, I myself am a huge fan of pirates and would absolutely love more pirate-based video games, but Uncharted 4 never featured that awe factor like entering Shambala for the first time in Uncharted 2. Although I wouldn't call Uncharted 4: A Thief's End the best game Naughty Dog has made, it is definitely one of the most mechanically impressive titles to date, and I honestly can't see any other release beating it as my pick for game of the year. If any of you have yet to play this magnificent game, you are truly missing out.

    The Next Few Months

    2016 has been an astounding year for video games thus far, and there are still dozens of big releases still to come. While I'm not personally very excited for the more juggernaut titles like Final Fantasy 15, Gears of War 4 or Call of Duty: Infinite Warfare, I am incredibly excited for games like The Last Guardian, ReCore, Pokemon Moon and Gravity Rush 2. After seeing the surprisingly impressive gameplay from E3, I'm even finding myself excited for the release of Watch Dogs 2. There really is a game for every kind of gamer on the horizon. The next few months will surely be monumental to this industry, and I can't wait to get my hands on all of these immensely promising titles.
